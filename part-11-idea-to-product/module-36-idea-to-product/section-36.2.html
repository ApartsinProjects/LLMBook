Choosing the Model's Role

"They promoted me from 'spell checker' to 'autonomous agent' on a Monday. By Wednesday I had booked fourteen flights, cancelled a lease, and apologized to someone's mother-in-law. I am back to spell checking now."

Deploy The Production Realist

Prerequisites

This section builds on the product mindset from Section 36.1. It assumes familiarity with prompt engineering (Chapter 11), AI agents (Chapter 22), and evaluation (Chapter 29). Readers who have also covered AI strategy (Chapter 33) will find the cross-references to cost and organizational readiness especially useful.

Big Picture

The single most consequential design decision in any AI product is how much autonomy to give the model. Most AI features fail not because the model is incapable, but because it was cast in the wrong role. A model asked to make autonomous decisions in a high-stakes domain will produce spectacular failures. The same model, cast as a drafter whose output a human reviews, becomes a productivity multiplier. This section introduces the copilot-to-autopilot spectrum, five concrete role patterns, and a reusable AI Role Canvas that you can fill out before writing a single line of code.

1. The Copilot-to-Autopilot Spectrum Basic

Every AI feature sits somewhere on a spectrum of autonomy. At one end, the model is a passive assistant that suggests but never acts. At the other end, the model operates independently, making decisions and executing actions without human review. Most successful AI products cluster toward the copilot end of this spectrum, and for good reason.

The spectrum breaks into four zones:

  1. Suggestion only. The model proposes; the human decides and acts. Examples: autocomplete, grammar suggestions, code completions. The human retains full control and the model's errors are cheap to ignore.
  2. Draft and review. The model produces a complete first draft; the human edits and approves before it reaches the end user. Examples: email drafts, report generation, content summarization. The human is a quality gate.
  3. Supervised automation. The model acts on its own within defined boundaries, but a human monitors and can intervene. Examples: ticket routing with escalation rules, content moderation with appeal paths. The human is an exception handler.
  4. Full autonomy. The model decides and executes without human involvement. Examples: spam filtering, real-time ad bidding, simple FAQ responses. This works only when the cost of individual errors is very low and the volume makes human review impractical.
Key Insight

Start at the copilot end and earn your way toward autopilot. Teams that launch with full autonomy often discover failure modes they never anticipated. Teams that start with "draft and review" collect real-world data on where the model fails, build targeted guardrails for those failure modes, and then selectively expand autonomy in areas where the model has proven reliable. This incremental approach aligns with the evaluation-first philosophy from Chapter 29: you cannot safely increase autonomy without the metrics to justify it.

2. Five Role Patterns Intermediate

Within the autonomy spectrum, five recurring role patterns appear across successful AI products. Each pattern defines what the model does, where the human fits, and what the typical failure modes look like. Combining these patterns in a pipeline (classifier into router into drafter, for example) is how production systems handle complex workflows while keeping each step simple and testable.

2.1 Drafter

The model generates a first-pass artifact (text, code, image, plan) that a human then edits, refines, and publishes. This is the most common and safest pattern. The prompt engineering techniques from Chapter 11 are especially relevant here, since draft quality depends directly on prompt design.

  • Examples: GitHub Copilot (code drafts), Notion AI (writing drafts), email reply suggestions.
  • Human role: Editor and approver. The human brings domain expertise and final judgment.
  • Key risk: Automation complacency. Over time, humans rubber-stamp drafts without reading them carefully.

2.2 Classifier

The model assigns inputs to predefined categories. Classification is one of the most reliable uses of LLMs because the output space is constrained and evaluation is straightforward: you can measure accuracy, precision, and recall against labeled test sets.

  • Examples: Support ticket routing, content tagging, sentiment analysis, intent detection.
  • Human role: Exception handler. The human reviews low-confidence classifications and edge cases.
  • Key risk: Distribution shift. The categories that worked at launch may not cover new types of input that emerge over time.

2.3 Router

The model selects which tool, API, or downstream process to invoke based on the input. This pattern is central to agentic architectures (Chapter 22), where the agent's primary job is deciding which tool to call rather than generating the final answer itself.

  • Examples: Function calling in chat interfaces, API gateway selection, workflow branching.
  • Human role: Designer and auditor. The human defines the available routes and reviews routing logs.
  • Key risk: Misrouting. A wrong tool call can trigger irreversible side effects (sending an email, deleting a record).

2.4 Researcher

The model retrieves, synthesizes, and summarizes information from one or more sources. The human consumes the summary and makes decisions based on it. This pattern benefits heavily from RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) and the evaluation strategies in Chapter 29 for measuring retrieval quality and faithfulness.

  • Examples: Legal research assistants, competitive analysis tools, literature review bots.
  • Human role: Critical consumer. The human validates sources and checks for hallucinated citations.
  • Key risk: Hallucinated sources. The model may fabricate references or misrepresent what a source actually says.

2.5 Verifier

The model checks output (its own or another system's) against defined criteria. Verification is powerful because it is often easier than generation: it is harder to write a correct legal contract than to check whether a given contract contains a specific clause.

  • Examples: Code review bots, compliance checkers, fact-checking pipelines, output validators.
  • Human role: Arbiter on disagreements. When the verifier flags an issue, a human makes the final call.
  • Key risk: False negatives. The verifier misses a genuine problem, creating a false sense of safety.
Fun Fact

GitHub Copilot, one of the most commercially successful AI products, has a suggestion acceptance rate of roughly 30%. That means the model is "wrong" (or at least unhelpful) 70% of the time, yet the product generates billions of dollars in annual revenue. The lesson: a product where the model is wrong most of the time can still be enormously valuable, as long as the role (suggestion) matches the reliability level. If Copilot were an autopilot that committed code without review, that 70% miss rate would be catastrophic.

Practical Example: From Autopilot Back to Copilot

A legal-tech startup initially built a contract review tool as an autonomous agent: the model read contracts, identified risky clauses, and rewrote them automatically. Lawyers rejected the product because they could not trust unsupervised rewrites of legal language. The team pivoted to a verifier pattern: the model highlights potentially risky clauses and cites the relevant regulation, but the lawyer makes all edits. Adoption increased from 12% to 78% within three months. The model did less, but the product did more. This is the pattern the strategy chapter (Chapter 33) calls "minimum viable autonomy."

4. The AI Role Canvas Intermediate

Before writing any code, fill out an AI Role Canvas: a one-page template that forces you to make explicit decisions about the model's responsibilities, boundaries, and operating constraints. The canvas prevents the common failure mode where a team starts coding before agreeing on what the model should (and should not) do.

The canvas has seven fields:

  1. Task description. A single sentence describing what the model does. If you cannot state it in one sentence, the scope is probably too broad.
  2. Human checkpoint. Where in the workflow a human reviews, approves, or overrides the model's output. Even fully autonomous features benefit from periodic human audits.
  3. Fallback behaviour. What happens when the model fails, times out, or returns low-confidence results. A good fallback is never "crash silently." Typical fallbacks include routing to a human, returning a safe default, or displaying a transparent error message.
  4. Acceptable error rate. The maximum failure rate your use case can tolerate. A spam filter at 2% false positives is fine; a medical diagnosis tool at 2% false negatives is not. This field links directly to the evaluation thresholds you set in Chapter 29.
  5. Latency budget. The p95 latency ceiling in milliseconds. A code completion must respond in under 200ms; a research summary can take 10 seconds. The latency budget often determines which model tier is feasible.
  6. Cost ceiling. The maximum cost per interaction in dollars. This constraint, discussed further in Chapter 33, shapes model selection, prompt length, and whether you can afford multi-step reasoning.
  7. Data sources and privacy constraints. What data the model can access, what it must never see (PII, credentials, internal secrets), and how data flows through the system.

The following Python class implements the AI Role Canvas as a structured configuration object that you can version-control alongside your codebase. 

# AI Role Canvas: a structured configuration for defining model responsibilities
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from enum import Enum
from typing import Optional


class RolePattern(Enum):
    """The five standard role patterns for AI components."""
    DRAFTER = "drafter"
    CLASSIFIER = "classifier"
    ROUTER = "router"
    RESEARCHER = "researcher"
    VERIFIER = "verifier"


@dataclass
class AIRoleCanvas:
    """One-page specification for an AI component's role and constraints.

    Fill this out before writing any model integration code.
    Version-control it alongside your prompts and evaluation suites.
    """

    # --- What the model does ---
    task_description: str            # One sentence: what does the model do?
    role_pattern: RolePattern        # Primary role pattern
    secondary_roles: list[RolePattern] = field(default_factory=list)

    # --- Human oversight ---
    human_checkpoint: str = ""       # Where does a human review output?
    fallback_behaviour: str = ""     # What happens when the model fails?

    # --- Quality and cost boundaries ---
    acceptable_error_rate: float = 0.05   # Max tolerable failure rate (0.0 to 1.0)
    latency_budget_ms: int = 3000         # p95 latency ceiling in milliseconds
    cost_ceiling_usd: float = 0.05        # Max cost per interaction in USD

    # --- Data governance ---
    data_sources: list[str] = field(default_factory=list)
    pii_allowed: bool = False             # Can the model see PII?
    privacy_notes: str = ""               # Additional privacy constraints

    # --- Metadata ---
    owner: str = ""                       # Team or person responsible
    version: str = "0.1.0"               # Canvas version for change tracking
    last_reviewed: Optional[str] = None   # ISO date of last review

    def summary(self) -> str:
        """Return a human-readable summary for documentation and logs."""
        roles = [self.role_pattern.value]
        roles.extend(r.value for r in self.secondary_roles)
        return (
            f"Task: {self.task_description}\n"
            f"Roles: {', '.join(roles)}\n"
            f"Human checkpoint: {self.human_checkpoint}\n"
            f"Fallback: {self.fallback_behaviour}\n"
            f"Error budget: {self.acceptable_error_rate:.1%}\n"
            f"Latency budget: {self.latency_budget_ms}ms (p95)\n"
            f"Cost ceiling: ${self.cost_ceiling_usd:.3f}/interaction\n"
            f"PII allowed: {self.pii_allowed}\n"
            f"Owner: {self.owner} | Version: {self.version}"
        )


# --- Example: a support-ticket classifier ---
ticket_classifier = AIRoleCanvas(
    task_description="Classify incoming support tickets by intent and priority.",
    role_pattern=RolePattern.CLASSIFIER,
    human_checkpoint="Agent reviews all 'urgent' classifications before escalation.",
    fallback_behaviour="Route to general queue if confidence < 0.7.",
    acceptable_error_rate=0.08,       # 8% misclassification is tolerable
    latency_budget_ms=500,            # Must respond within 500ms p95
    cost_ceiling_usd=0.002,           # ~$0.002 per classification
    data_sources=["zendesk_tickets", "product_taxonomy_v3"],
    pii_allowed=True,                 # Tickets contain customer names and emails
    privacy_notes="PII is masked before logging. No ticket content stored beyond 90 days.",
    owner="support-platform-team",
    version="1.2.0",
    last_reviewed="2026-03-15",
)

print(ticket_classifier.summary())

The example instantiates a canvas for a support-ticket classifier with explicit error budgets, latency ceilings, and privacy constraints.
Practical Example: Filling Out the Canvas for a Legal Research Assistant

Suppose you are building an AI tool that helps lawyers research case precedents. Here is how you might fill out the canvas:

  • Task: "Retrieve and summarize relevant case law for a given legal question."
  • Role pattern: Researcher (primary), Drafter (secondary, for the summary).
  • Human checkpoint: Lawyer reviews every summary and verifies every cited case before using it in a filing.
  • Fallback: If retrieval returns fewer than three relevant cases, display a warning and suggest the lawyer broaden the search terms manually.
  • Error rate: 0% tolerance for fabricated citations (hallucinated case names). Up to 10% tolerance for missing a relevant case that exists in the corpus.
  • Latency: 10,000ms p95 (lawyers expect research to take time).
  • Cost: $0.15 per query (justified by the high value of lawyer time saved).
  • Data sources: Internal case database, court records API. No access to privileged client communications.

Notice how the canvas forces you to think about failure modes and human oversight before you choose a model or write a prompt. This aligns with the evaluation-first approach from Chapter 29: the canvas defines what "success" looks like, which is exactly what your evaluation suite needs to measure.

5. From Canvas to Implementation Advanced

A filled-out canvas translates directly into engineering decisions. The role pattern determines your prompt structure. The human checkpoint determines your UI flow. The error budget determines your evaluation thresholds. The latency and cost budgets determine your model tier. The privacy constraints determine your data pipeline.

The following code shows how the canvas drives model selection and prompt construction in practice. 

# Translating an AI Role Canvas into model selection and prompt construction
from typing import Any

# Model tier recommendations keyed by role pattern and latency budget
MODEL_RECOMMENDATIONS: dict[str, dict[str, str]] = {
    "classifier": {
        "fast": "gpt-4o-mini",        # < 1s latency budget
        "standard": "gpt-4o",          # 1s to 5s latency budget
    },
    "drafter": {
        "fast": "claude-3-5-haiku",
        "standard": "claude-sonnet-4",
    },
    "router": {
        "fast": "gpt-4o-mini",
        "standard": "gpt-4o",
    },
    "researcher": {
        "fast": "claude-sonnet-4",
        "standard": "claude-opus-4",    # Deeper reasoning for research
    },
    "verifier": {
        "fast": "gpt-4o-mini",
        "standard": "gpt-4o",
    },
}

# Role-specific system prompt templates
ROLE_PROMPTS: dict[str, str] = {
    "drafter": (
        "You are a drafting assistant. Produce a complete first draft "
        "that the user will review and edit. Prioritize coverage and "
        "clarity over perfection."
    ),
    "classifier": (
        "You are a classification engine. Assign the input to exactly "
        "one of the provided categories. Return ONLY the category name "
        "and a confidence score between 0.0 and 1.0."
    ),
    "router": (
        "You are a routing engine. Given the user's request, select "
        "the most appropriate tool or API from the provided list. "
        "Return ONLY the tool name and your reasoning in one sentence."
    ),
    "researcher": (
        "You are a research assistant. Retrieve relevant information, "
        "synthesize it into a clear summary, and cite your sources "
        "explicitly. Never fabricate a source or citation."
    ),
    "verifier": (
        "You are a verification engine. Check the provided output "
        "against the given criteria. List any violations found with "
        "evidence. If no violations exist, state that explicitly."
    ),
}


def select_model(canvas: Any) -> str:
    """Choose a model tier based on the canvas constraints.

    Uses role pattern and latency budget to find the best fit
    from MODEL_RECOMMENDATIONS.
    """
    role = canvas.role_pattern.value
    tier = "fast" if canvas.latency_budget_ms < 1000 else "standard"
    recommendations = MODEL_RECOMMENDATIONS.get(role, {})
    return recommendations.get(tier, "gpt-4o")  # Safe default


def build_system_prompt(canvas: Any) -> str:
    """Generate a system prompt tailored to the canvas role and constraints.

    Appends privacy and fallback rules as explicit constraints.
    """
    base = ROLE_PROMPTS.get(canvas.role_pattern.value, "")
    constraints: list[str] = []

    if not canvas.pii_allowed:
        constraints.append("Never include or reference personal information.")
    if canvas.fallback_behaviour:
        constraints.append(f"Fallback rule: {canvas.fallback_behaviour}")
    if canvas.privacy_notes:
        constraints.append(f"Privacy: {canvas.privacy_notes}")

    if constraints:
        base += "\n\nConstraints:\n" + "\n".join(f"- {c}" for c in constraints)

    return base


# --- Use the ticket_classifier canvas from Code Fragment 36.2.1 ---
model = select_model(ticket_classifier)
system_prompt = build_system_prompt(ticket_classifier)
print(f"Selected model: {model}")
print(f"\nSystem prompt:\n{system_prompt}")
Code Fragment 36.2.2: The canvas drives concrete engineering choices. select_model picks a model tier based on latency and role constraints, while build_system_prompt generates role-specific instructions with embedded guardrails from the canvas's privacy and fallback fields.

6. Common Mistakes in Role Assignment Intermediate

Three patterns of failure recur when teams assign roles to their AI components:

  1. The "do everything" anti-pattern. The model is asked to classify, research, draft, and verify all in a single prompt. This produces unreliable results because each role requires a different prompt structure, different evaluation criteria, and different failure handling. Instead, decompose the workflow into discrete steps, each with its own role and canvas. The agent architectures in Chapter 22 provide patterns for composing multiple roles into a pipeline.
  2. Premature autopilot. The team skips the copilot phase and launches with full autonomy because the demo looked good. As Section 36.1 demonstrated, demo performance does not predict production performance. Start with human-in-the-loop, measure the model's real-world error rate, and only remove the human checkpoint when the data justifies it.
  3. Missing fallback. The canvas's "fallback behaviour" field is left blank or filled with "N/A." Every model call can fail: the API times out, the response is malformed, the confidence is too low. Without an explicit fallback, these failures propagate to the user as crashes, blank screens, or (worse) silent hallucinations.
Warning: The Autonomy Ratchet

Once users grow accustomed to a copilot workflow, there is organizational pressure to "just let the AI handle it" and remove the human checkpoint. Resist this pressure unless your evaluation metrics clearly show the model can handle the increased autonomy. Increasing autonomy without increasing monitoring is a recipe for the kind of silent quality degradation described in Section 36.1.

Key Takeaways

  • The copilot-to-autopilot spectrum defines your product's risk profile. Start at the copilot end, collect data on real-world failure modes, and expand autonomy only where metrics justify it.
  • Five role patterns cover most AI use cases. Drafter, classifier, router, researcher, and verifier each have distinct prompt structures, evaluation criteria, and failure modes. Combine them in pipelines for complex workflows.
  • The AI Role Canvas is a pre-coding deliverable. Seven fields (task description, human checkpoint, fallback behaviour, acceptable error rate, latency budget, cost ceiling, data sources and privacy) force explicit decisions before you write a single line of integration code.
  • Decompose rather than aggregate. A single model trying to fill every role produces unreliable results. Break workflows into discrete steps, each with its own role and canvas.
  • Fallback behaviour is not optional. Every model call can fail. An explicit fallback (route to human, return default, show warning) prevents silent failures from reaching users.

1. A team wants to build an AI feature that automatically sends refund emails to customers without human review. Where does this sit on the copilot-to-autopilot spectrum, and what questions should they answer before launching?
Show Answer
This is full autonomy (zone 4 on the spectrum). Before launching, the team should answer: What is the acceptable error rate for incorrect refunds? What is the cost of a wrongly sent refund email (financial and reputational)? What is the fallback if the model generates an incorrect amount or sends to the wrong customer? What monitoring is in place to detect drift? In most cases, the risk profile suggests starting with "draft and review" (zone 2), where the model composes the email and a human approves it before sending.
2. Name the five role patterns and give one sentence explaining when each is most appropriate.
Show Answer
(1) Drafter: use when you need a first-pass artifact that a human will refine. (2) Classifier: use when inputs must be sorted into a fixed set of categories. (3) Router: use when the model's job is selecting which tool or process to invoke. (4) Researcher: use when the model must retrieve and synthesize information from sources. (5) Verifier: use when the model checks output against defined criteria rather than generating new content.
3. What are the seven fields of the AI Role Canvas, and why is the "fallback behaviour" field especially important?
Show Answer
The seven fields are: (1) task description, (2) human checkpoint, (3) fallback behaviour, (4) acceptable error rate, (5) latency budget, (6) cost ceiling, and (7) data sources and privacy constraints. Fallback behaviour is especially important because every model call can fail (API timeout, low confidence, malformed response). Defining the fallback forces the team to design graceful degradation paths before they encounter failures in production.
