Safety, Ethics & Regulation

"With great power comes great responsibility. The same technology that can democratize access to knowledge can also amplify harm at unprecedented scale."

A Responsible AI Agent

Chapter Overview

With the production engineering foundations from Chapter 26 in place, this module tackles the safety, ethical, and regulatory dimensions of deploying LLMs at scale. It covers the OWASP Top 10 for LLMs, prompt injection defenses, hallucination detection and mitigation, bias measurement, model cards, and environmental impact.

Building on the alignment techniques covered in Chapter 16, the regulatory landscape (EU AI Act, GDPR, US executive orders) and enterprise governance frameworks (NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001) are examined alongside practical audit strategies. The module concludes with licensing, intellectual property, privacy, and the emerging field of machine unlearning, preparing the ground for the strategic and ROI considerations in Chapter 28.

Learning Objectives

  • Defend against OWASP Top 10 LLM threats including prompt injection, jailbreaking, and data exfiltration
  • Detect and mitigate hallucinations using self-consistency, citation verification, and constrained generation, complementing interpretability methods from Chapter 17
  • Measure and reduce bias in LLM outputs through systematic auditing and model cards
  • Navigate the EU AI Act, GDPR, and US regulatory frameworks for AI governance
  • Implement enterprise risk governance using NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, and SR 11-7 frameworks
  • Understand model licensing taxonomies, IP ownership, and differential privacy for LLM training data
  • Apply machine unlearning techniques for GDPR compliance, copyright removal, and safety alignment

Sections

  • 27.1 LLM Security Threats 🟡 ⚙️ 🔧 OWASP Top 10 for LLMs, prompt injection, jailbreaking defenses, input sanitization, sandwich defense, output scanning, and PII redaction strategies.

    • Practical Example: Prompt Injection at a Healthcare Startup

    Who: A digital health startup using an LLM to summarize patient intake forms for physicians.

    Situation: The system accepted free-text patient descriptions and generated structured summaries for clinical review.

    Problem: A security researcher demonstrated that a carefully crafted "symptom description" could instruct the model to ignore the system prompt and instead output fabricated medical histories, potentially leading to misdiagnosis.

    Dilemma: Aggressive input filtering risked stripping legitimate medical terminology, while permissive filtering left the system vulnerable.

    Decision: The team implemented a layered defense: input classification with Prompt Guard, a sandwich defense wrapping user content with reinforced instructions, and an NLI-based output verifier that flagged summaries inconsistent with the original input.

    Result: Injection success rate dropped from 23% to under 0.1%, with no measurable impact on legitimate summary quality.

    Lesson: In high-stakes domains, defense in depth is not optional. No single technique stops all prompt injection; layered approaches are essential.

  • 27.2 Hallucination & Reliability 🟡 ⚙️ Types of hallucination, detection methods (self-consistency, citation verification, NLI), mitigation strategies (RAG, constrained generation, confidence calibration), and abstention.
  • 27.3 Bias, Fairness & Ethics 🟡 ⚙️ Sources of bias in LLMs, measurement techniques, fairness metrics, model cards, datasheets for datasets, and environmental impact of large-scale training.

    • Practical Example: When the Model Card Saved the Project

    Who: A financial services firm deploying an LLM for automated loan application screening.

    Situation: The model showed strong overall accuracy (92%) on internal benchmarks and was fast-tracked for production deployment.

    Problem: During a mandatory model card review, the fairness audit team discovered that performance dropped to 74% accuracy for applicants from rural zip codes and non-native English speakers, creating a disparate impact risk.

    Decision: The team paused deployment, augmented training data with underrepresented demographics, added fairness constraints to the evaluation pipeline, and published an updated model card documenting known limitations and recommended use conditions.

    Result: The revised model achieved 89% accuracy overall with less than 3% variance across demographic groups, satisfying both regulatory requirements and internal fairness standards.

    Lesson: Model cards are not bureaucratic paperwork. They are a forcing function that catches bias before it reaches real users. Skipping the card skips the safety net.

  • 27.4 Regulation & Compliance 🟡 ⚙️ EU AI Act risk tiers, GDPR requirements for AI, US executive orders, sector-specific regulations (HIPAA, finance, education), and building AI governance programs.
  • 27.5 LLM Risk Governance & Audit 🔴 ⚙️ Enterprise model inventory, risk classification frameworks, SR 11-7 model risk management, NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, and audit trail implementation.

    • Practical Example: Surviving the EU AI Act Audit

    Who: A European SaaS company offering an AI-powered hiring tool classified as "high-risk" under the EU AI Act.

    Situation: With the Act's compliance deadline approaching, the company needed to demonstrate conformity across transparency, human oversight, data governance, and risk management requirements.

    Problem: The engineering team had no centralized model inventory, no audit trail for prompt changes, and no documented risk assessment. Compliance felt like an impossible retrofit.

    Decision: They adopted the NIST AI RMF as their internal framework, built a model registry tracking every deployed version, implemented ISO 42001-aligned documentation, and established a quarterly audit cadence with both automated and manual reviews.

    Result: The company passed its first conformity assessment with minor findings. More importantly, the governance infrastructure they built became a competitive advantage, as enterprise customers increasingly required evidence of AI risk management.

    Lesson: Regulatory compliance is not just a legal checkbox. Treating governance as a product feature, rather than an afterthought, creates lasting business value (Chapter 28).

    The "Right to Be Forgotten" Meets LLMs

    Under GDPR, individuals can request deletion of their personal data. But what happens when that data has been absorbed into the weights of a 70-billion-parameter model? You cannot simply search the parameters for "John Smith" and delete those neurons. This paradox gave rise to the field of machine unlearning, which Section 27.7 covers in detail. Early approaches were so aggressive they occasionally caused models to forget how to write grammatically correct English, proving that surgical forgetting in neural networks remains one of the hardest open problems in AI.

  • 27.6 LLM Licensing, IP & Privacy 🟡 ⚙️ Model license taxonomy, commercial use considerations, IP ownership of LLM outputs, training data copyright, anonymization techniques, and differential privacy.
  • 27.7 Machine Unlearning 🔴 ⚙️ Unlearning methods (gradient ascent, LOKA, task vectors), evaluation of forgetting quality, and GDPR/copyright/safety motivations for selective knowledge removal. Relates to alignment (Chapter 16).

Bibliography

Foundational Papers

  1. OWASP Foundation (2025). "OWASP Top 10 for Large Language Model Applications." owasp.org/www-project-top-10-for-large-language-model-applications

    The definitive security risk taxonomy for LLM applications, covering prompt injection, data poisoning, and supply chain vulnerabilities.

  2. Perez, E., Huang, S., Song, F., Cai, T., Ring, R., Aslanides, J., Glaese, A., McAleese, N., & Irving, G. (2022). "Red Teaming Language Models with Language Models." arXiv:2202.03286

    Introduces automated red teaming using LLMs to discover failure modes in other LLMs, a key technique for safety testing.

  3. Mitchell, M., Wu, S., Zaldivar, A., Barnes, P., Vasserman, L., Hutchinson, B., Spitzer, E., Raji, I. D., & Gebru, T. (2019). "Model Cards for Model Reporting." arXiv:1810.03993

    Proposes the model card framework for transparent documentation of model performance, limitations, and intended use.

  4. Bender, E. M., Gebru, T., McMillan-Major, A., & Shmitchell, S. (2021). "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big?" ACM FAccT 2021

    Influential paper examining the environmental costs, bias risks, and societal harms of large language models.

  5. Jang, J., Yoon, D., Yang, S., Cha, S., Lee, M., Logeswaran, L., & Seo, M. (2023). "Knowledge Unlearning for Mitigating Language Models' Risks." arXiv:2210.01504

    Explores machine unlearning methods for selectively removing knowledge from LLMs to address privacy and safety concerns.

Key Books & Standards

  1. NIST (2023). "Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0)." nist.gov/artificial-intelligence

    The U.S. federal framework for managing AI risks, providing governance structures and measurement approaches.

  2. European Parliament (2024). "EU Artificial Intelligence Act." artificialintelligenceact.eu

    The world's first comprehensive AI regulation, establishing risk tiers and compliance requirements for AI systems deployed in the EU.
