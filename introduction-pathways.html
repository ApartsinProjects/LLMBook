Suggested Course Pathways

With 31 chapters, no single semester can cover everything. The following four pathways are designed for instructors adopting this book for a single-semester (14-week) university course. Each pathway selects chapters and labs that fit the semester's time constraints while maintaining pedagogical coherence. All pathways assume a 3-hour weekly session (lecture plus lab). If you are a self-directed learner rather than an instructor, skip ahead to the Self-Study Pathways section below.

Pathway 1: Undergraduate Engineering

Focus: Foundations, using LLM APIs, building basic agents. Students leave able to build and deploy LLM-powered applications. This pathway spends the first five weeks on foundations because undergraduates typically lack exposure to attention mechanisms and tokenization; skipping this material would leave them unable to debug prompt failures or understand why a model generates unexpected output. The second half jumps to the applied chapters (APIs, RAG, agents) because the goal is practical competency, not research depth.

Pathway 2: Undergraduate Research

Focus: Architecture internals, training methods, interpretability. Students leave with a deep understanding of how LLMs work and how to study them. This pathway trades breadth for depth: it covers the same foundations as Pathway 1 but then dives into pre-training, scaling laws, PEFT, and alignment rather than APIs and agents. The reasoning is that future researchers need to understand the training pipeline end to end, since that is where novel contributions happen. Interpretability (Chapter 18) appears last because it requires fluency with model internals from all the preceding chapters.

Pathway 3: Graduate Engineering

Focus: Full stack from APIs through production deployment. Students leave able to architect, build, and operate LLM systems at scale. This pathway assumes students already have solid ML fundamentals, so it starts with a transformer review and quickly moves to the practitioner stack. The ordering (inference optimization before APIs, fine-tuning before RAG, agents before production) mirrors the typical system design sequence: you must understand model capabilities and costs before you can design a reliable architecture around them.

Pathway 4: Graduate Research

Focus: Training, alignment, scaling, interpretability, and frontier topics. Students leave prepared to conduct original research in LLM science. This pathway is the most technically demanding. It opens with a deep dive into attention and decoding because graduate researchers need to reason about architectural modifications at the level of individual operations. The middle weeks build the full training stack (pre-training, fine-tuning, distillation, alignment) in the order a research lab would encounter them when developing a new model. The final week on research frontiers synthesizes everything into a forward-looking perspective, preparing students to identify open problems worth pursuing.

Self-Study Pathways

Not everyone will read this book cover to cover, and that is by design. The following pathways are tailored to specific goals and backgrounds. Each one identifies the chapters to focus on, chapters to skip or skim, and a realistic time estimate for a reader dedicating 8 to 12 hours per week.

1. "I Want to Build AI Products" (Product Manager / Startup Founder)

Estimated time: 4 to 6 weeks

Skip: Chapters 0 through 5 (foundations), Chapter 18 (interpretability), Chapters 13 through 17 (training and alignment).

Skim: Chapters 6 through 9 (enough to understand capabilities and limitations).

Focus on: Ch 10: APIs, Ch 11: Prompt Engineering, Ch 12: Hybrid Architectures, Ch 20: RAG, Ch 22: Agents, Ch 28: Applications, Ch 29: Evaluation, Ch 31: Production, Ch 32: Safety, Ch 33: Strategy and ROI.

Goal: Understand what LLMs can and cannot do, how to build products around them, and how to evaluate cost, quality, and risk.

2. "I Want to Fine-Tune and Train Models" (ML Engineer)

Estimated time: 6 to 8 weeks

Start at: Chapter 6 (if already comfortable with transformer fundamentals; otherwise start at Chapter 3).

Focus on: Ch 6: Pre-training and Scaling, Ch 7: Model Landscape, Ch 9: Inference Optimization (including Section 09.6: Test-Time Compute), Ch 13: Synthetic Data, Ch 14: Fine-Tuning, Ch 15: PEFT, Ch 16: Distillation, Ch 17: Alignment, Ch 29: Evaluation.

Goal: Master the full training pipeline from data preparation through alignment, with practical experience on real hardware.

3. "I Want to Build AI Agents" (Software Engineer)

Estimated time: 4 to 6 weeks

Prerequisites: Review Chapters 0 and 1 if new to ML. Otherwise start at Chapter 10.

Focus on: Ch 10: APIs, Ch 11: Prompt Engineering, Ch 19: Embeddings, Ch 20: RAG, Ch 21: Conversational AI, Ch 22: AI Agents, Ch 23: Multi-Agent Systems, Ch 28: Applications, Ch 29: Evaluation.

Goal: Build autonomous agents that use tools, maintain state, coordinate with other agents, and interact with users through modern protocols (MCP, AG-UI).

4. "I Want to Deploy LLMs in Production" (Platform / DevOps Engineer)

Estimated time: 3 to 4 weeks

Focus on: Ch 9: Inference Optimization, Ch 10: APIs, Ch 20: RAG (infrastructure sections), Ch 29: Evaluation and Observability, Ch 31: Production Engineering and LLMOps, Ch 32: Safety, Ethics, and Security (especially Section 32.1 on security), Ch 33: Strategy and ROI.

Skim: Chapters 6 and 7 for context on model capabilities and costs.

Goal: Understand how to serve, monitor, scale, and secure LLM-powered systems in production environments.

5. "I'm a Researcher Exploring LLMs" (PhD Student / Researcher)

Estimated time: 10 to 12 weeks

Approach: Read the full book, with particular emphasis on the Research Frontier sections at the end of each chapter.

Key chapters: Ch 4: Transformer Architecture, Ch 6: Scaling Laws, Ch 7: Model Architectures, Section 09.6: Test-Time Compute, Ch 18: Interpretability, Ch 13: Synthetic Data, Ch 17: Alignment.

Goal: Develop both theoretical depth and practical fluency. Use the annotated bibliographies as entry points into the research literature.

6. "I'm Completely New to ML" (Career Changer)

Estimated time: 16 to 20 weeks

Approach: Start at Chapter 00 and read sequentially. Do not skip chapters. Complete every lab exercise; the hands-on practice is essential for building genuine understanding.

Pace: Plan for roughly two chapters per week through Part 1, then one chapter per week for the remaining material. Do not rush the foundations.

Goal: Build a complete mental model of the LLM stack, from basic linear algebra through production agent systems, with working code at every step.

7. "I'm a Data Scientist Adding LLMs to My Toolkit" (Data Scientist / Analyst)

Estimated time: 5 to 7 weeks

You already know: Python, pandas, scikit-learn, basic deep learning. You want to integrate LLMs into existing data workflows.

Skim: Chapters 0 through 2 (refresh if needed). Chapter 4 (transformer internals, unless curious).

Focus on: Ch 3: Attention, Ch 5: Text Generation, Ch 10: APIs, Ch 11: Prompt Engineering (including Section 11.3 on DSPy/AutoML), Ch 12: Hybrid ML+LLM (your bridge chapter, with Section 12.1 on AutoML integration), Ch 19: Embeddings and Vector DBs, Ch 20: RAG, Ch 28: Applications (Text-to-SQL, automated analytics), Ch 29: Evaluation.

Goal: Learn where LLMs complement (not replace) your existing ML models, how to build hybrid pipelines, and how to use LLMs for feature engineering, classification, extraction, and analytics.

8. "I'm an NLP Engineer Transitioning to LLMs" (NLP / Text Mining Professional)

Estimated time: 4 to 5 weeks

You already know: Classical NLP (spaCy, NLTK, BERT fine-tuning, NER, text classification). You want to understand what changes with LLMs.

Skip: Chapters 0 through 2 (you know this). Chapter 3 (review attention if needed).

Focus on: Ch 4: Transformer Architecture (deeper than BERT), Ch 5: Decoding Strategies, Ch 6: Scaling Laws (why scale matters), Ch 10: LLM APIs, Ch 11: Prompt Engineering, Ch 12: Hybrid ML+LLM (Section 12.5: Structured Extraction), Ch 14: Fine-Tuning, Ch 15: PEFT/LoRA, Ch 17: Alignment, Ch 20: RAG.

Goal: Understand how LLMs change the NLP landscape, when to use prompting vs. fine-tuning vs. classical NLP, and how to migrate existing pipelines to LLM-augmented architectures.

9. "I'm a Full-Stack Developer Adding AI Features" (Web / App Developer)

Estimated time: 3 to 4 weeks

You already know: JavaScript/Python, REST APIs, databases, web frameworks. You do not need to understand model internals.

Skip: All of Part 1 (Chapters 0 through 5), Part 2 (Chapters 6 through 9), and Part 4 (Chapters 13 through 18).

Focus on: Ch 10: LLM APIs (your starting point), Ch 11: Prompt Engineering, Ch 19: Embeddings (for search), Ch 20: RAG (for grounding responses in your data), Ch 21: Conversational AI (for chat features), Ch 22: Agents (for tool use and automation), Ch 28: Applications (Text-to-SQL, recommendations, search), Ch 31: Production Engineering.

Goal: Add AI-powered features (chat, search, extraction, generation) to your applications using APIs and frameworks, without needing to train any models.

10. "I'm a Technical Leader Evaluating LLM Strategy" (CTO / Tech Lead / Architect)

Estimated time: 2 to 3 weeks

You need: Enough depth to make informed build-vs-buy decisions, evaluate vendor claims, and guide your team. You do not need to write model code.

Skim: Ch 4: Transformer Architecture (concepts only), Ch 6: Scaling Laws (cost implications), Ch 7: Model Landscape (choosing models), Ch 9: Inference Optimization (cost and latency).

Focus on: Ch 10: APIs (vendor comparison), Ch 11: Prompt Engineering (what your team needs to know), Ch 20: RAG (the most common enterprise pattern), Ch 29: Evaluation (how to measure quality), Ch 31: Production (infrastructure decisions), Ch 32: Safety and Compliance, Ch 33: Strategy, Product, and ROI.

Goal: Develop a mental model for LLM capabilities and limitations, understand cost structures, make architectural decisions, and evaluate vendor solutions with confidence.

11. "I'm a Domain Expert Applying LLMs" (Healthcare / Legal / Finance Professional)

Estimated time: 4 to 5 weeks

You have: Deep domain expertise but limited ML background. You want to apply LLMs to your specific field.

Start at: Chapter 00 (foundations) if unfamiliar with ML concepts, otherwise Chapter 10.

Focus on: Ch 10: APIs, Ch 11: Prompt Engineering (your primary tool), Ch 12: Hybrid ML+LLM (Section 12.5: Information Extraction for your domain), Ch 14: Fine-Tuning (adapting models to your terminology), Ch 20: RAG (grounding in your knowledge base), Ch 28: Applications (healthcare in 28.3, finance in 28.2, legal in 28.6, robotics and embodied AI in Section 28.7), Ch 32: Safety, Ethics, and Compliance (critical for regulated industries).

Goal: Build LLM applications tailored to your domain while navigating regulatory requirements, ensuring accuracy, and integrating with existing professional workflows.

12. "I Want to Understand AI Safety and Alignment" (Safety Researcher / Policy Analyst)

Estimated time: 5 to 6 weeks

Prerequisites: Read Chapters 3 and 4 for transformer fundamentals.

Focus on: Ch 6: Scaling Laws (emergent capabilities), Section 09.6: Test-Time Compute (reasoning model safety implications), Ch 17: Alignment (RLHF, DPO, Constitutional AI), Ch 18: Interpretability (mechanistic understanding, sparse autoencoders, circuit tracing), Ch 29: Evaluation (measuring safety), Ch 32: Safety, Ethics, and Regulation (all 7 sections, with expanded security depth in Section 32.1), plus the Research Frontier callouts throughout.

Skim: Ch 7: Model Landscape (safety profiles of different models), Ch 23: Multi-Agent Systems (emergent behaviors).

Goal: Understand the technical mechanisms behind LLM safety challenges, current alignment approaches, interpretability tools, and the regulatory landscape.

13. "I Want to Build RAG and Search Systems" (Search / Knowledge Engineer)

Estimated time: 4 to 5 weeks

You already know: Information retrieval basics, databases, or search infrastructure.

Focus on: Ch 1: Text Representation (classical foundations), Ch 19: Embeddings and Vector Databases (all sections), Ch 20: RAG (all 6 sections, your core chapter), Section 28.4: Recommendation and Search, Ch 29: Evaluation (Section 29.3: RAG evaluation).

Supplement with: Ch 10: APIs (for LLM integration), Ch 11: Prompt Engineering (query formulation), Ch 12: Hybrid ML+LLM (combining retrieval with ML).

Goal: Master the full retrieval pipeline from document processing through embedding, indexing, retrieval, reranking, and generation, with production evaluation methods.

14. "I Want to Contribute to Open-Source LLM Projects" (Open-Source Developer)

Estimated time: 8 to 10 weeks

Approach: You need both theoretical depth (to understand what you are changing) and practical breadth (to navigate large codebases).

Core path: Ch 0: PyTorch Foundations, Ch 4: Transformer Architecture (implement from scratch), Ch 5: Decoding (generation pipeline), Ch 6: Pre-training, Ch 7: Model Landscape (architecture differences), Ch 9: Inference Optimization (KV cache, quantization, speculative decoding, and Section 09.6: Test-Time Compute), Ch 14: Fine-Tuning, Ch 15: PEFT, Ch 18: Interpretability (internals access).

Supplement with: Ch 16: Distillation and Merging, Ch 17: Alignment. Review the labs and code fragments closely; contributing to projects like vLLM, llama.cpp, or Hugging Face Transformers requires fluency with these concepts.

Goal: Understand LLM internals deeply enough to contribute meaningful code to training frameworks, inference engines, and model libraries.

15. "I'm a Hobbyist Building Weekend Projects" (Curious Tinkerer)

Estimated time: 3 to 4 weeks (pick and choose)

You want: To build fun, useful things with LLMs without committing to a full curriculum.

Start with: Ch 10: APIs (get something working in 30 minutes), Ch 11: Prompt Engineering (make it work well).

Then pick your adventure:

  • Build a chatbot: Ch 21: Conversational AI, Section 21.3: Memory Management
  • Build a personal knowledge base: Ch 19: Embeddings, Ch 20: RAG
  • Build an AI agent: Ch 22: AI Agents, Section 22.2: Tool Use
  • Analyze your data: Section 20.5: Text-to-SQL, Section 28.4: Recommendation and Search
  • Fine-tune a small model: Ch 14: Fine-Tuning, Ch 15: PEFT/LoRA

Goal: Get from idea to working prototype as fast as possible, then go deeper in whichever direction excites you most.

Mixing Pathways

These pathways are suggestions, not prescriptions. Many readers will combine elements of multiple pathways. An ML engineer who also wants to build agents might follow Pathway 2 first, then pivot to the agent chapters from Pathway 3. A data scientist exploring safety might pair Pathway 7 with Pathway 12. The cross-references throughout the book make it easy to jump between chapters while maintaining context.
