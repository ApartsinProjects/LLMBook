Chapter Overview

Imagine deploying a medical AI that recommends a treatment, and a doctor asks, "Why this recommendation?" You open the model's attention weights and find it fixated on a patient's zip code, not their symptoms. Without interpretability tools, you would never have caught this failure. As large language models are deployed in high-stakes applications, the question "why did the model produce this output?" becomes critical. Interpretability research aims to open the black box of transformer models, revealing the internal computations that drive predictions, the features that neurons encode, and the circuits that implement specific behaviors.

This module covers the full spectrum of interpretability methods for transformers. It begins with attention analysis and probing classifiers, which offer accessible entry points for understanding model internals. It then advances to mechanistic interpretability, the ambitious program of reverse-engineering neural networks at the level of individual features and circuits. The module also covers practical interpretability tools for debugging, model editing, and representation engineering, as well as formal attribution methods for explaining transformer predictions.

By the end of this module, you will be able to analyze attention patterns to understand model behavior, use probing classifiers to test what information is encoded in hidden states, apply sparse autoencoders to extract interpretable features, and employ attribution methods to explain individual predictions.

Practical Example: Anthropic's Sparse Autoencoder Breakthrough

Who: The Anthropic interpretability team (2023).

Situation: Researchers wanted to understand what individual neurons in Claude actually represent, but most neurons were polysemantic, responding to multiple unrelated concepts.

Problem: Traditional neuron-level analysis was unreliable because a single neuron might activate for both "Golden Gate Bridge" and "the color orange," making any interpretation misleading.

Dilemma: The team could either accept neuron-level analysis as inherently noisy or invest months developing a new decomposition method with no guarantee it would yield cleaner features.

Decision: They trained sparse autoencoders (SAEs) on the model's activations to decompose polysemantic neurons into monosemantic features.

How: SAEs learned a larger, overcomplete set of features with a sparsity penalty, so each feature fired only for a single coherent concept (e.g., one feature for "Golden Gate Bridge" alone).

Result: They extracted millions of interpretable features from Claude, including features for specific cities, programming concepts, emotional tones, and safety-relevant behaviors. Activating the "Golden Gate Bridge" feature caused the model to mention the bridge in every response.

Lesson: The right decomposition method can transform an opaque network into a catalogue of interpretable concepts; the features were always there, just entangled in superposition.

Practical Example: Medical AI Bias Caught by Attention Analysis

Who: A machine learning team at a hospital network piloting an AI diagnostic assistant (2022).

Situation: The model was recommending different treatment pathways for patients with identical symptoms, and clinicians noticed the pattern correlated with patient demographics.

Problem: Without interpretability tools, the team had no way to confirm whether the model was using clinical features or demographic proxies to make decisions.

Dilemma: Pulling the system offline would delay patient care improvements; leaving it running risked perpetuating bias.

Decision: They applied attention visualization and integrated gradients to trace which input tokens most influenced the model's recommendations.

How: The team ran attribution analysis on hundreds of cases, comparing which input fields received the highest attribution scores across demographic groups.

Result: The analysis confirmed the model was placing significant attention weight on zip code fields, which served as a proxy for race and income. The team masked those features and retrained, eliminating the disparity.

Lesson: Interpretability is not a luxury for research papers; it is a prerequisite for deploying AI in settings where errors carry real human consequences.

Trying to understand what a neural network has learned by looking at individual neurons is a bit like trying to understand a symphony by listening to one musician at a time. You hear notes, certainly, but the music only emerges when you understand how they play together. Interpretability researchers are, in effect, learning to read the orchestral score.

    • Practical Example: The Logit Lens Reveals GPT-2's Internal "Draft Predictions"

    Who: Nostalgebraist, an independent ML researcher (2020).

    Situation: Researchers knew that transformer layers progressively refine representations, but had no simple way to see what prediction each intermediate layer would make if it were the final layer.

    Problem: Understanding when and where the model "decides" on its output was impossible without running the full forward pass and examining only the final logits.

    Dilemma: Building elaborate probes for each layer would be expensive and might not capture how predictions evolve across depth.

    Decision: The researcher applied the unembedding matrix directly to intermediate hidden states, projecting each layer's representation into vocabulary space.

    How: For each layer, the hidden state was multiplied by the output embedding matrix to produce a probability distribution over tokens, revealing what the model would predict at that depth.

    Result: The technique showed that early layers often predict broad semantic categories (e.g., "a country name"), while later layers sharpen to the specific token (e.g., "France"). Some predictions stabilize by the middle layers; others flip dramatically in the final few.

    Lesson: Sometimes the most powerful interpretability tools are the simplest. A single matrix multiplication revealed the progressive refinement process that defines how transformers think.

    • Practical Example: ROME Edits a Model's Factual Knowledge Without Retraining

    Who: Kevin Meng and collaborators at MIT (2022).

    Situation: A language model confidently stated that the Eiffel Tower is in London. Retraining on corrected data to fix a single fact would cost thousands of dollars in compute.

    Problem: Factual errors in deployed models are inevitable, but full retraining for each correction is impractical. Fine-tuning risks catastrophic forgetting of other knowledge.

    Dilemma: The team needed to surgically edit one fact without degrading the model's performance on everything else.

    Decision: They developed Rank-One Model Editing (ROME), which locates the specific MLP layer storing a factual association and overwrites that single weight update.

    How: By tracing causal effects with activation patching, they identified that mid-layer MLPs act as key-value stores for factual knowledge. ROME computes a rank-one update to the relevant weight matrix that changes exactly one factual association.

    Result: The edit successfully changed the model's answer to "Paris" for all paraphrases of the question, while leaving unrelated knowledge untouched. Subsequent work (MEMIT) scaled this to thousands of simultaneous edits.

    Lesson: Interpretability research does not just explain models; it enables precise interventions that would be impossible without understanding the internal structure of how knowledge is stored.

    Practical Example: OpenAI's Induction Head Discovery

    Who: Catherine Olsson and colleagues at Anthropic (2022).

    Situation: Researchers observed that transformer language models developed in-context learning abilities during training, but the mechanism was unknown.

    Problem: In-context learning (the ability to learn from examples in the prompt) appeared suddenly during training, suggesting a discrete computational mechanism rather than a gradual statistical trend.

    Dilemma: The team could treat in-context learning as an emergent black-box capability or invest in painstaking circuit-level analysis to find the responsible components.

    Decision: They performed detailed mechanistic analysis, tracing information flow through attention heads across layers.

    How: By examining two-layer attention patterns, they identified "induction heads": circuits where one attention head copies information to a token, and a second head uses that copied information to predict what comes next in repeated sequences.

    Result: They found that induction heads form during a specific phase transition in training, and their emergence precisely coincides with the sharp improvement in in-context learning ability. This was one of the first concrete mechanistic explanations for an emergent capability.

    Lesson: Emergent capabilities need not remain mysterious. Patient circuit-level analysis can reveal the specific mechanisms responsible, transforming "magic" into engineering understanding.

Mechanistic interpretability researchers sometimes describe their work as "neuroscience for artificial brains." The key difference: when a neuroscientist wants to know what a particular brain region does, they cannot simply clone the brain and delete that region. Interpretability researchers can, and do, exactly that. The models never complain, though they do occasionally start speaking nonsense.
