Chapter Overview

A language model learns a probability distribution over sequences of tokens, but that distribution alone does not produce text. The bridge between a trained model and the words it generates is the decoding strategy: the algorithm that selects which token comes next (or, in newer paradigms, which tokens appear all at once). The choice of decoding method profoundly affects quality, diversity, coherence, speed, and even the safety of generated output.

This chapter walks through the full landscape of text generation, from the simplest deterministic methods (greedy search, beam search) through stochastic sampling techniques (temperature, top-k, top-p, min-p) to advanced and emerging approaches (contrastive decoding, speculative decoding, structured generation, watermarking, and diffusion-based language models). By the end, you will understand not just what each method does, but when and why to choose one over another.

Practical Example: The Chatbot That Loved Repetition

Who: A product team at a travel booking startup with 4M monthly active users.

Situation: They deployed a customer support chatbot using greedy decoding to keep responses deterministic and "safe."

Problem: Users reported that the bot kept repeating the same phrases: "I'd be happy to help you with that. I'd be happy to help you with that." Greedy decoding had fallen into a repetition loop on roughly 18% of longer conversations.

Dilemma: Switching to sampling risked generating off-topic or hallucinated travel advice; keeping greedy decoding meant continued user frustration.

Decision: They adopted nucleus (top-p) sampling at p=0.9 combined with a repetition penalty of 1.2, giving the model enough freedom to vary phrasing while constraining wild outputs.

How: A/B testing over two weeks on 50,000 conversations compared the old greedy decoder against the new sampling configuration.

Result: Repetition complaints dropped by 81%. User satisfaction scores rose from 3.2 to 4.1 out of 5, with no measurable increase in factual errors.

Lesson: Determinism is not the same as quality. A well-tuned sampling strategy can be both diverse and reliable.

Greedy decoding is like always ordering the most popular dish at every restaurant you visit. You will never have a terrible meal, but you will also never discover that incredible hole-in-the-wall ramen place three blocks off the main road.

Practical Example: Structured JSON or Bust

Who: A backend engineering team at a health-tech company building an intake form auto-filler.

Situation: Their LLM extracted patient symptoms from free-text notes and returned structured JSON for downstream processing by the electronic health record (EHR) system.

Problem: Roughly 12% of responses contained malformed JSON (missing closing braces, trailing commas, unquoted keys), causing silent failures in the ingestion pipeline.

Dilemma: They could add a post-processing JSON repair layer (fragile, edge-case-heavy), or enforce valid JSON at generation time.

Decision: They integrated grammar-constrained decoding using a JSON schema that matched their EHR data model, ensuring every generated token was structurally valid.

How: Using the Outlines library, they compiled their JSON schema into a finite-state machine that masked illegal tokens at each decoding step.

Result: Malformed JSON dropped to 0%. Pipeline reliability went from 88% to 99.7%, and the team eliminated 400 lines of brittle post-processing code.

Lesson: When your application demands structured output, do not rely on the model's goodwill. Constrain the decoding process itself.

Practical Example: Speculative Decoding Saves the Latency Budget

Who: An infrastructure team at a legal document analysis firm serving 200 enterprise clients.

Situation: Their 70B-parameter model produced excellent contract summaries but took 8 seconds per response, well above the 3-second SLA demanded by their largest client.

Problem: Quantization alone brought latency down to 5.5 seconds, still too slow. Switching to a smaller model degraded summary quality below acceptable thresholds.

Dilemma: They needed the quality of the large model at the speed of a small one, without purchasing additional GPU capacity.

Decision: They implemented speculative decoding, using a 7B draft model to propose token sequences that the 70B verifier model accepted or rejected in parallel.

How: The draft model generated 5 candidate tokens per step. The verifier checked all 5 in a single forward pass, accepting an average of 3.8 tokens per cycle.

Result: End-to-end latency dropped to 2.9 seconds (a 2.7x speedup) with mathematically identical output quality, since rejected tokens were resampled from the large model's distribution.

Lesson: Speculative decoding offers a rare free lunch: faster inference with zero quality loss, as long as the draft model is a reasonable approximation.

Practical Example: Watermarking for Academic Integrity

Who: The academic integrity office at a large public university (45,000 students).

Situation: After a surge in AI-generated essay submissions, the university needed a way to distinguish AI-written text from student-written text without relying on unreliable third-party detectors.

Problem: Existing detection tools produced false positive rates of 15 to 20%, unfairly flagging legitimate student work and eroding trust in the system.

Dilemma: They could ban AI tools entirely (unenforceable), invest in better detectors (costly and still imperfect), or work with their internal LLM provider to embed statistical watermarks.

Decision: They partnered with their LLM vendor to enable watermarking via the "green list" approach, subtly biasing token selection toward a pseudorandom subset detectable by a secret key.

How: The watermark was invisible to readers but statistically detectable with a p-value below 0.001 on passages longer than 200 tokens.

Result: Detection accuracy reached 97% on watermarked text with a false positive rate under 1%. The university could now distinguish AI-assisted drafts from fully AI-generated submissions.

Lesson: Watermarking shifts the detection problem from heuristic guessing to cryptographic verification, but it only works when you control the generation endpoint.

Temperature in language models works exactly like temperature in cooking. Too low and everything comes out the same bland flavor. Too high and your kitchen is on fire. The art is finding the setting where things are interesting but not catastrophic.
