Chapter Overview

Every modern AI system that can read, write, or converse began with the ideas in this chapter.

How do machines learn to read? This chapter traces the evolution of text representation from counting words to understanding meaning. Building on the neural network and optimization fundamentals from Module 0: ML & PyTorch Foundations, we start with the fundamental challenge of turning raw human language into numbers, work through classical techniques like Bag-of-Words and TF-IDF, then explore the revolution sparked by Word2Vec and dense word embeddings.

Along the way, you will build a complete text preprocessing pipeline, train word embeddings from scratch, explore the famous king/queen analogy, and see how contextual embeddings (ELMo) paved the road to the transformer models covered in Module 4: Transformer Architecture. Understanding this progression is essential: the entire history of NLP is a quest for better representations of meaning, and each technique you learn here is a building block for everything that follows.

Practical Example: When Bag-of-Words Met Sarcasm

Who: A junior NLP engineer at a mid-size e-commerce company (12M monthly reviews)

Situation: The team built a product sentiment classifier using TF-IDF features to flag negative reviews for urgent response.

Problem: The classifier tagged "Oh sure, this product is just AMAZING" as positive. Sarcastic reviews were slipping through at a rate of roughly 23%, causing angry customers to wait days for follow-up.

Dilemma: They could add hand-crafted sarcasm rules (fast but brittle), or invest weeks switching to word embeddings that capture semantic nuance.

Decision: They replaced TF-IDF with pre-trained Word2Vec embeddings, reasoning that dense vectors would capture tonal patterns that sparse counts could not.

How: They fine-tuned 300-dimensional Word2Vec vectors on their review corpus, then trained a simple logistic regression on the averaged embeddings.

Result: Sarcasm misclassification dropped from 23% to 9%. Customer response SLA compliance rose from 74% to 91% within two months.

Lesson: Representation quality is the ceiling for downstream model performance. Upgrading from sparse to dense embeddings often delivers bigger gains than swapping classifiers. For production-scale embedding pipelines, see Module 18: Embeddings & Vector Databases.

Practical Example: The Polysemy Trap in Medical Search

Who: A search engineer at a healthcare information startup serving 400K monthly queries

Situation: Their search system used GloVe embeddings to match patient queries ("my child has a cold") to medical articles.

Problem: The word "cold" had a single static embedding that blended its medical meaning (common cold) with its temperature meaning. Queries about feeling cold returned articles about the flu, and queries about cold weather returned medical advice.

Dilemma: They could build separate domain-specific embeddings (expensive, months of work) or explore the newer contextual embedding approach (ELMo), which was less battle-tested in production.

Decision: They adopted ELMo embeddings, which generate a different vector for "cold" depending on surrounding words.

How: They replaced their GloVe-based query encoder with a fine-tuned ELMo model, keeping the same retrieval pipeline and re-indexing their 50K article corpus.

Result: Relevant-result click-through rate jumped from 34% to 52%. Polysemy-related support tickets dropped by 60% in the first quarter.

Lesson: When your domain has words with multiple critical meanings, static embeddings will silently degrade search quality. Contextual embeddings are not a luxury; they are a necessity. Modern transformer-based approaches (see Module 4: Transformer Architecture) take this idea even further with full self-attention.

Practical Example: Preprocessing Saves a Recommendation Engine

Who: A data scientist at an online bookstore building a "similar books" feature

Situation: They trained TF-IDF vectors on 200K book descriptions and used cosine similarity to recommend similar titles.

Problem: Recommendations were nonsensical. A thriller about a bank heist was matched to a personal finance guide. Investigation revealed that raw text included HTML tags, publisher boilerplate, and inconsistent casing, all of which polluted the feature space.

Dilemma: Rush to a fancier model (Word2Vec, neural retrieval), or invest a week cleaning the preprocessing pipeline first.

Decision: They built a proper pipeline: HTML stripping, lowercasing, lemmatization, stop-word removal, and boilerplate detection.

How: Using spaCy, they wrote a five-stage pipeline that reduced the average document from 1,200 tokens to 180 meaningful lemmas, then retrained the same TF-IDF model.

Result: User engagement with recommendations rose 41%. The "thumbs-down" rate on suggestions fell from 38% to 14%, all without changing the underlying algorithm.

Lesson: Before reaching for a more powerful model, audit your preprocessing. Clean input often outperforms a fancy model fed noisy data. For a deeper look at subword tokenization techniques that modern LLMs use, see Module 2: Tokenization & Subword Models.

Word2Vec's famous "king minus man plus woman equals queen" analogy is the NLP equivalent of a magic trick: genuinely impressive, but do not try "lawyer minus ethics plus ambition" at home.

Bibliography & Further Reading

Foundational Papers

  1. Mikolov, T. et al. (2013). "Efficient Estimation of Word Representations in Vector Space." arxiv.org/abs/1301.3781

    The original Word2Vec paper introducing Skip-gram and CBOW, which demonstrated that dense word vectors capture semantic relationships.

  2. Pennington, J., Socher, R., & Manning, C. D. (2014). "GloVe: Global Vectors for Word Representation." EMNLP 2014. nlp.stanford.edu/projects/glove

    Introduces GloVe embeddings, which combine global co-occurrence statistics with local context window methods.

  3. Bojanowski, P. et al. (2017). "Enriching Word Vectors with Subword Information." TACL, 5, 135–146. arxiv.org/abs/1607.04606

    Presents FastText, which extends Word2Vec by representing words as bags of character n-grams, enabling embeddings for unseen words.

  4. Peters, M. E. et al. (2018). "Deep contextualized word representations." NAACL 2018. arxiv.org/abs/1802.05365

    Introduces ELMo, the first widely adopted contextual embedding model that generates different vectors for the same word in different contexts.

  5. Harris, Z. S. (1954). "Distributional Structure." Word, 10(2-3), 146–162. doi.org/10.1080/00437956.1954.11659520

    The foundational linguistics paper articulating the distributional hypothesis: words that occur in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings.

Key Books

  1. Jurafsky, D. & Martin, J. H. (2024). Speech and Language Processing (3rd ed. draft). web.stanford.edu/~jurafsky/slp3

    The standard NLP textbook, with excellent chapters on text preprocessing, n-gram models, and vector semantics.

  2. Manning, C. D. & Schütze, H. (1999). Foundations of Statistical Natural Language Processing. MIT Press. nlp.stanford.edu/fsnlp

    A classic reference for understanding the statistical underpinnings of NLP, including TF-IDF, collocations, and information retrieval.

  3. Goldberg, Y. (2017). Neural Network Methods for Natural Language Processing. Morgan & Claypool. doi.org/10.2200/S00762ED1V01Y201703HLT037

    A concise guide bridging classical NLP and neural approaches, covering word embeddings and feed-forward networks for text classification.

Tools & Libraries

  1. spaCy: Industrial-Strength Natural Language Processing. spacy.io

    A production-ready NLP library for tokenization, lemmatization, named entity recognition, and text preprocessing pipelines.

  2. NLTK: Natural Language Toolkit. nltk.org

    The classic Python NLP library for educational purposes, providing corpora, stemmers, and text processing utilities.

  3. Gensim: Topic Modelling for Humans. radimrehurek.com/gensim

    A Python library for training Word2Vec, FastText, and other embedding models, with efficient similarity queries and corpus handling.

  4. Mikolov, T. et al. (2013). "Distributed Representations of Words and Phrases and their Compositionality." NeurIPS 2013. arxiv.org/abs/1310.4546

    The follow-up Word2Vec paper introducing negative sampling and phrase-level embeddings, with practical training improvements.
