Chapter Overview

This is the central module of the entire course. The Transformer, introduced in the landmark 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need," is the architecture behind every modern large language model. In this chapter we will dissect it layer by layer, build one from scratch, survey the many variants that have emerged since, understand the GPU hardware it runs on, and explore the theoretical limits of what Transformers can and cannot compute.

By the end of this module you will be able to read a Transformer implementation, modify it confidently, reason about its computational cost, and understand why certain architectural choices (positional encoding, layer normalization, residual connections) are not arbitrary but deeply principled.

Practical Example: Google Translate's Transformer Migration

Who: The Google Translate engineering team (2017).

Situation: Google Translate had been running on a sequence-to-sequence LSTM architecture that processed tokens one at a time, making training slow and parallelism limited.

Problem: Training times for new language pairs stretched to weeks, and translation quality plateaued on long sentences where the LSTM's sequential bottleneck lost context.

Dilemma: Rewriting the entire production pipeline for an unproven architecture risked months of engineering effort with uncertain payoff.

Decision: The team committed to migrating the core model to the Transformer architecture described in "Attention Is All You Need."

How: They replaced the recurrent encoder and decoder with stacked self-attention layers, enabling full parallelism across sequence positions during training.

Result: Training time dropped dramatically (from weeks to days for comparable models), and BLEU scores improved across nearly every language pair, especially for longer sentences.

Lesson: The Transformer's ability to attend to all positions simultaneously was not just a theoretical improvement; it translated directly into faster iteration and better quality at scale.

Learning Objectives

  • Walk through the original Transformer paper and explain every component from positional encodings to output probabilities
  • Implement a complete decoder-only Transformer in ~300 lines of PyTorch, training it on a small dataset
  • Compare encoder-only, decoder-only, and encoder-decoder architectures with concrete use cases
  • Explain efficient attention mechanisms (linear attention, sparse attention, FlashAttention) and their tradeoffs
  • Describe State Space Models (SSMs/Mamba), Mixture-of-Experts (MoE), RWKV, Gated Attention, and Multi-head Latent Attention
  • Understand GPU architecture (SMs, memory hierarchy, bandwidth) and write a basic Triton kernel
  • State the universal approximation and computational complexity results for Transformers, and explain how chain-of-thought reasoning extends their power

If the Transformer were a cooking recipe, the ingredient list would just say "attention" seven times, followed by "layer norm" and "pray to the GPU gods." The fact that this recipe produces coherent poetry, working code, and passable legal advice remains one of the great surprises of modern computer science.

Sections

  • 4.1 Transformer Architecture Deep Dive 🔴 📐 Paper walkthrough, positional encoding (sinusoidal and learned), feed-forward networks, layer normalization (Pre-LN vs Post-LN), weight initialization, residual connections, information flow, and the complete forward pass.

    • Practical Example: Spotify's Pre-LN Discovery

    Who: A machine learning engineer at Spotify working on podcast transcription models (2021).

    Situation: The team was training a custom Transformer for multilingual podcast transcription, but training runs kept diverging after a few thousand steps.

    Problem: Loss spikes and NaN gradients appeared unpredictably, wasting expensive GPU hours and blocking the product launch.

    Dilemma: The team could reduce the learning rate (slowing convergence) or investigate architectural changes (risking further delays).

    Decision: They switched from Post-Layer Normalization to Pre-Layer Normalization, placing the LayerNorm before (rather than after) each sub-layer.

    How: A single architectural change: moving two lines of code per Transformer block so that normalization preceded self-attention and feed-forward layers.

    Result: Training stabilized immediately, allowing a 3x higher learning rate and cutting total training time by 40%. The model shipped on schedule.

    Lesson: Small architectural details like normalization placement can make or break a training run. Understanding each component of the Transformer is not academic; it is survival.

  • 4.2 Build a Transformer from Scratch 🔴 🧪 Implement a complete decoder-only Transformer (~300 lines of PyTorch). Covers token embeddings, causal masking, multi-head attention, feed-forward blocks, and a training loop on a character-level language modeling task.
  • 4.3 Transformer Variants & Efficiency 🔴 🌍 Encoder-only (BERT), decoder-only (GPT), encoder-decoder (T5). Efficient attention (linear, sparse, FlashAttention). State Space Models (Mamba), RWKV, Mixture-of-Experts, Gated Attention, Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA).

    • Practical Example: Mistral's Mixture-of-Experts Gamble

    Who: The Mistral AI team in Paris (2023).

    Situation: Mistral wanted to compete with models 10x their parameter count but had a fraction of the compute budget of larger labs.

    Problem: A dense 7B parameter model was good but could not match the quality of 70B dense models on reasoning benchmarks.

    Dilemma: Training a larger dense model was financially impossible. They needed a way to scale capacity without proportionally scaling compute.

    Decision: They adopted a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture, routing each token through only 2 of 8 expert feed-forward networks per layer.

    How: A learned gating network selected the top-2 experts per token, keeping inference cost similar to the 7B dense model while expanding total parameters to 46.7B.

    Result: Mixtral 8x7B matched or exceeded LLaMA 2 70B on most benchmarks while using roughly 6x less inference compute per token.

    Lesson: Architectural innovation (specifically, understanding Transformer variants like MoE) can compensate for compute disadvantages. Knowing the design space is a strategic asset.

  • 4.4 GPU Fundamentals & Systems 🟡 🧪 Streaming multiprocessors, memory hierarchy (registers, shared memory, HBM), roofline model, FlashAttention algorithm, Triton programming, and a lab implementing a fused softmax kernel.

    • Practical Example: FlashAttention Saves a Startup's Runway

    Who: A three-person NLP startup building a legal document analysis tool (2023).

    Situation: Their fine-tuned model needed to process contracts up to 16,000 tokens long, but standard attention consumed so much GPU memory that they could only run batch size 1 on their rented A100.

    Problem: Inference was too slow (12 seconds per document) for their real-time product demo, and investors were visiting in two weeks.

    Dilemma: Renting more GPUs would burn through remaining funding. Truncating documents would miss critical contract clauses.

    Decision: They integrated FlashAttention-2, which restructures the attention computation to minimize memory reads and writes by tiling the operation in SRAM.

    How: A single library swap (replacing the default PyTorch attention with FlashAttention) required minimal code changes but fundamentally changed the memory access pattern.

    Result: Memory usage dropped by 60%, enabling batch size 8. Inference time fell to 1.8 seconds per document. The demo impressed investors.

    Lesson: Understanding GPU memory hierarchy (Section 4.4) is not just for hardware engineers. A single systems-level optimization saved months of runway for this team.

    Theorists proved that Transformers live in complexity class TC0, which means they cannot even sort a list in a single forward pass. And yet, somehow, they write better cover letters than most humans. The gap between theoretical limitations and practical capabilities remains one of the field's most entertaining contradictions.

  • 4.5 Transformer Expressiveness Theory 🟠 🔬 Universal approximation for sequence-to-sequence functions, Transformers as computational models (TC0, log-precision), limitations on inherently serial problems, and how chain-of-thought reasoning extends computational power.
