Prompting Reasoning & Multimodal Models

How prompting strategies change for reasoning models, multimodal prompt patterns, and designing effective prompts for o1/o3/R1 versus standard models

"The art of prompting a reasoning model is knowing when to stop helping it think."

A Restraint-Practicing AI Agent

Prerequisites

This section builds on the core prompting techniques from Section 10.1: Prompt Design Fundamentals and the advanced patterns in Section 10.2: Advanced Prompting Techniques. Familiarity with reasoning model APIs from Section 9.4: Reasoning Models & Multimodal APIs is strongly recommended, as this section focuses on the prompting layer rather than the API mechanics.

💡 Key Insight

Mental Model: The Expert Consultant. Prompting a standard model is like giving detailed step-by-step instructions to a junior employee: you spell out every intermediate step because the employee will not figure them out independently. Prompting a reasoning model is like briefing a senior consultant: you describe the problem clearly and the constraints precisely, then let the expert figure out the approach. Over-specifying the reasoning steps for a reasoning model is counterproductive; it constrains the model's internal thinking to follow your (possibly suboptimal) decomposition rather than discovering its own, potentially better reasoning path.

1. How Prompting Changes for Reasoning Models

The single most important shift when prompting reasoning models is this: stop writing chain-of-thought instructions. Standard models benefit enormously from explicit "think step by step" prompts because they generate tokens left to right without any planning phase. Reasoning models (whose API mechanics are covered in Section 9.4) already have a dedicated thinking phase; adding chain-of-thought instructions creates redundancy at best and interference at worst.

1.1 What to Remove from Your Prompts

Standard Model Prompt PatternReasoning Model AdjustmentWhy
"Think step by step"Remove entirelyThe model already thinks step by step internally
"First analyze X, then consider Y, finally..."State the goal, not the stepsOver-specifying steps constrains the model's reasoning
"Show your work"Remove (thinking trace is automatic)Reasoning trace is returned separately in the API response
"Let's break this down"RemoveDecomposition happens in the thinking phase
Few-shot examples with CoTUse examples without CoT, or zero-shotCoT examples can anchor the model to a specific reasoning style

1.2 What to Emphasize Instead

With reasoning models, your prompt budget is better spent on three things: (1) a precise problem statement, (2) clear constraints and edge cases, and (3) the desired output format. The model's internal thinking will handle the reasoning decomposition. Code Fragment 1 below contrasts how the same acquisition-analysis task would be prompted for a standard model versus a reasoning model.

# Compare standard vs. reasoning model prompt design
# Standard models need explicit CoT scaffolding; reasoning models need problem clarity
# Notice how the reasoning prompt is shorter but more information-dense
#
# STANDARD MODEL PROMPT (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet without thinking)
# Needs explicit chain-of-thought scaffolding
standard_prompt = """
Analyze whether Company X should acquire Company Y.

Think through this step by step:
1. First, evaluate the strategic fit (market overlap, capabilities)
2. Then, assess the financial implications (price, synergies, debt)
3. Next, consider integration risks (culture, technology, retention)
4. Finally, provide your recommendation with confidence level

Company X: [details]
Company Y: [details]
"""

# REASONING MODEL PROMPT (o3, Claude with extended thinking)
# Focus on problem clarity and constraints, not reasoning steps
reasoning_prompt = """
Should Company X acquire Company Y?

Context:
Company X: [details]
Company Y: [details]

Constraints to consider:
- X cannot take on more than $500M in additional debt
- Regulatory approval in the EU is uncertain (40-60% probability)
- X's board requires 18-month payback on acquisition premium

Provide a recommendation (acquire / pass / conditional) with the
key factors driving your conclusion.
"""
Code Fragment 1: Chain-of-thought prompting that instructs the model to reason step by step. Explicit reasoning steps improve accuracy on multi-step problems.

1.3 When Reasoning Models Underperform Standard Models

Reasoning models are not universally better. They can underperform standard models in several situations:

  • Simple classification tasks: For sentiment analysis, spam detection, or topic labeling, the reasoning overhead adds latency and cost without improving accuracy. A standard model with a good prompt is faster and cheaper.
  • Creative writing: The internal thinking phase can make reasoning models overly analytical and cautious in creative tasks, producing prose that reads as "well-reasoned" rather than "inspired."
  • High-volume, low-complexity tasks: Processing thousands of simple extractions (names, dates, amounts) is better served by a fast, cheap model like GPT-4o-mini or Claude Haiku.
  • Tasks with explicit CoT in the prompt: If your prompt already contains detailed step-by-step instructions, a reasoning model may conflict with your specified reasoning path, producing worse results than a standard model that follows instructions literally.
⚠ The Over-Prompting Trap

A common mistake when migrating from standard to reasoning models is keeping all the chain-of-thought scaffolding from the original prompt. This often degrades performance because the model's thinking phase tries to reconcile your prescribed reasoning steps with its own analysis, leading to confused or overly constrained outputs. When switching to a reasoning model, start by stripping your prompt down to the bare problem statement and constraints, then add back only what is necessary.

2. Multimodal Prompting Patterns

Multimodal prompts combine text with images, documents, or audio. The prompting challenge is guiding the model to attend to the right parts of the visual or audio input and produce the desired output format. Unlike text-only prompts where the model reads everything sequentially, multimodal models must integrate information from different modalities, and the quality of this integration depends heavily on how you frame the task.

2.1 Image + Text Prompting

The most effective pattern for image understanding is: describe what you want extracted, then provide the image. Placing the text instruction before the image gives the model a "lens" through which to process the visual input. For complex images (charts, diagrams, screenshots), include specific guidance about what to focus on. Code Fragment 2 demonstrates two common multimodal prompt patterns: structured data extraction from a chart and side-by-side comparison of two images.

# Multimodal prompting patterns for image analysis with Claude
# Pattern 1: structured extraction from a chart image
# Pattern 2: multi-image comparison with explicit labeling
import anthropic, base64

client = anthropic.Anthropic()

def encode_image(path):
    return base64.standard_b64encode(open(path, "rb").read()).decode()

# PATTERN 1: Structured extraction from a chart
response = client.messages.create(
    model="claude-sonnet-4-20250514",
    max_tokens=2048,
    messages=[{
        "role": "user",
        "content": [
            # Text instruction FIRST (gives the model a lens)
            {
                "type": "text",
                "text": "This is a bar chart showing quarterly revenue. "
                        "Extract each quarter's value and calculate the "
                        "quarter-over-quarter growth rate. Return as JSON."
            },
            # Image SECOND
            {
                "type": "image",
                "source": {
                    "type": "base64",
                    "media_type": "image/png",
                    "data": encode_image("revenue_chart.png")
                }
            }
        ]
    }]
)

# PATTERN 2: Multi-image comparison
response = client.messages.create(
    model="claude-sonnet-4-20250514",
    max_tokens=2048,
    messages=[{
        "role": "user",
        "content": [
            {"type": "text", "text": "Compare these two UI designs. "
                "Image 1 is the current version, Image 2 is the proposed "
                "redesign. Identify: (a) layout changes, (b) color "
                "changes, (c) potential accessibility concerns."},
            {"type": "image", "source": {"type": "base64",
                "media_type": "image/png",
                "data": encode_image("current_ui.png")}},
            {"type": "text", "text": "Image 1 (current) above. "
                "Image 2 (proposed) below."},
            {"type": "image", "source": {"type": "base64",
                "media_type": "image/png",
                "data": encode_image("proposed_ui.png")}}
        ]
    }]
)
Code Fragment 2: Anthropic Messages API call showing the distinct parameter layout. The system prompt is a top-level parameter rather than a message role, and max_tokens is required. Content blocks provide structured access to generated text.

2.2 Document Understanding Prompts

When sending PDF documents, the prompt design should account for the fact that the model sees each page as an image. For multi-page documents, explicitly tell the model whether information might span pages. For forms and tables, specify the exact fields to extract. For contracts and legal documents, point out which clauses or sections are most relevant.

2.3 Video Frame Analysis

Current models do not accept raw video, but you can extract key frames and send them as a sequence of images. The prompt should establish the temporal relationship between frames and guide the model to track changes across them.

📝 Token Costs of Multimodal Inputs

A single image consumes 85 to 1,700 tokens depending on resolution. A 10-page PDF can consume 8,000 to 17,000 tokens. When designing multimodal prompts, consider whether you need the full resolution or whether a downscaled image suffices. For batch processing, crop images to the relevant region before sending them to the API. This can reduce costs by 50% or more while often improving accuracy by removing distracting content.

3. Provider-Specific Prompt Strategies

Each reasoning model family has distinct characteristics that affect optimal prompt design. The differences are subtle but significant for production quality.

3.1 OpenAI o-Series (o1, o3, o4-mini)

  • System prompts: Supported but treated as "developer messages." Keep them short and focused on output format constraints rather than reasoning instructions.
  • Temperature: Fixed at 1.0 for o1; adjustable for o3 and o4-mini. The reasoning process itself provides consistency, so temperature has less impact than with standard models.
  • Best for: Math, coding, and structured analytical tasks. The o4-mini model offers good reasoning at lower cost for medium-complexity tasks.

3.2 Anthropic Claude (Extended Thinking)

  • System prompts: Fully supported and influential. Use the system prompt to define the role, constraints, and output format.
  • Thinking budget: Directly configurable. Start at 4,096 and increase based on task complexity. The model returns the full thinking trace, which you can use for debugging.
  • Best for: Nuanced analysis, writing that requires planning, and tasks where the thinking trace serves as an audit trail.

3.3 DeepSeek-R1

  • Thinking format: Thinking is embedded in the output between <think> and </think> tags. You parse the output to separate thinking from the answer.
  • Prompt sensitivity: R1 is more sensitive to prompt wording than other reasoning models. Small changes in phrasing can significantly alter the reasoning path.
  • Best for: Open-source deployment, research, and applications where you need full control over the model and reasoning process.
🎪 Fun Fact

When OpenAI first released o1, prompt engineers discovered that adding "Think step by step" to o1 prompts actually made it perform worse on math benchmarks. The model's internal reasoning was already more sophisticated than the human-written chain-of-thought, and the instruction forced it into a simpler reasoning pattern. This was the first widely observed case of a prompting technique having a negative transfer to a more capable model.

4. Practical Decision Framework

Use this framework to decide which model class and prompting strategy to use for a given task:

  1. Assess task complexity: If the task involves multi-step reasoning, mathematical proof, code architecture, or strategic analysis, consider a reasoning model. If it is classification, extraction, or creative generation, use a standard model.
  2. Evaluate modality needs: If the input includes images, documents, or audio, use a multimodal model. Place text instructions before visual inputs. For documents with complex layouts, prefer native PDF input over OCR.
  3. Design the prompt: For reasoning models, focus on problem clarity and constraints. For standard models, include chain-of-thought scaffolding and few-shot examples.
  4. Set the thinking budget: Start low (2,048 tokens) and increase only if the output quality is insufficient. Monitor the thinking token count to track cost.
  5. Iterate on output format: Reasoning models sometimes produce verbose outputs because their thinking phase generates thorough analysis. Add explicit format constraints ("respond in under 200 words" or "return only the JSON object") to keep outputs concise.

Key Takeaways

  1. Remove chain-of-thought scaffolding when switching to reasoning models. The model's internal thinking is more effective than human-prescribed reasoning steps.
  2. Focus on problem clarity and constraints rather than step-by-step instructions. Describe what you want, not how to think about it.
  3. Place text instructions before images in multimodal prompts to give the model a processing lens for visual inputs.
  4. Reasoning models are not always better: for simple classification, creative writing, and high-volume low-complexity tasks, standard models are faster, cheaper, and sometimes more effective.
  5. Each provider has distinct prompt sensitivities: OpenAI o-series prefers minimal system prompts, Claude benefits from detailed system context, and DeepSeek-R1 is sensitive to phrasing.
  6. Start with a low thinking budget and increase only when output quality is insufficient. Most tasks do not need maximum reasoning effort.
🏗️ Application Example

Migrating a Financial Analysis Pipeline from GPT-4o to o3

Who: A fintech startup's ML team building an automated due-diligence report generator for venture capital firms.

Situation: Their existing GPT-4o pipeline used a 2,000-token system prompt with detailed chain-of-thought instructions: "First analyze the revenue trend, then assess burn rate, then evaluate the competitive landscape, then..." The pipeline produced structured reports from pitch decks (sent as images) and financial spreadsheets.

Problem: When they switched to o3 without modifying the prompt, report quality dropped noticeably. The model's reasoning traces showed it was trying to follow the prescribed steps while simultaneously running its own analysis, producing redundant and sometimes contradictory sections.

Decision: They stripped the prompt from 2,000 tokens to 400 tokens: a clear problem statement ("Produce a due-diligence summary for a Series B investment"), explicit constraints (risk factors to flag, required output sections, maximum length), and the output JSON schema. All chain-of-thought scaffolding was removed.

Result: Report quality scores from analyst reviewers improved from 7.2/10 to 8.6/10. The shorter prompt also reduced input token costs by 80%. The reasoning traces showed the model discovered analytical angles that the original hand-crafted steps had missed, particularly around unit economics and customer concentration risk.

Lesson: When migrating to reasoning models, less prompting often produces better results; focus your prompt budget on problem clarity and constraints rather than reasoning scaffolding.

🔬 Research Frontier

The interaction between prompt design and reasoning model behavior is poorly understood. Early research suggests that reasoning models develop internal "prompting strategies" during their thinking phase, effectively re-prompting themselves. This raises the question of whether prompt engineering for reasoning models will converge toward simply stating the problem clearly, with all sophistication handled by the model's thinking process. Research on prompt-free reasoning (where the model determines the task from context alone) and thinking-aware prompting (where the prompt explicitly references the thinking phase) are active areas of exploration.
