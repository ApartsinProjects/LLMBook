Chapter 24: LLM Applications

LLM Applications Across Industries

"Every industry thinks it is special. Every industry is right. And every industry still needs the same four things from an LLM: understand my documents, talk to my customers, automate my workflows, and do not embarrass me."

Compass AI Agent

Prerequisites

This section surveys industry applications and draws on concepts from throughout Part 6. Familiarity with RAG pipelines, agent architectures, and the domain-specific applications in Sections 24.2 through 24.8 provides useful context.

Big Picture

LLM adoption is no longer limited to technology companies. Every major industry is deploying language models for document processing, customer interaction, compliance monitoring, and decision support. The common architectural patterns (RAG for knowledge retrieval, agents for workflow automation, fine-tuning for domain-specific language) recur across industries, but each sector brings its own regulatory constraints, data formats, latency requirements, and risk tolerances. This section provides a structured tour of eight industries, highlighting the key use case, a representative architecture, and the deployment considerations unique to each domain.

1. Agriculture: Crop Monitoring and Precision Farming

Agriculture is rapidly adopting vision-language models (VLMs) for crop health monitoring, pest identification, and yield prediction. Farmers and agronomists can photograph plants in the field and receive instant analysis of disease symptoms, nutrient deficiencies, or pest damage. Unlike traditional computer vision models that classify a fixed set of known conditions, VLMs can describe novel symptoms in natural language and suggest possible causes even for conditions not present in their training data.

Satellite and drone imagery combined with language models enables large-scale crop monitoring. An LLM agent can analyze multispectral imagery, correlate vegetation indices (NDVI, NDRE) with weather and soil data, and generate natural language reports for farm managers who lack remote sensing expertise. Yield prediction models combine historical yield data, weather forecasts, and satellite observations to generate field-level predictions with uncertainty estimates. 

# Crop health analysis with a vision-language model
from openai import OpenAI
import base64

client = OpenAI()

def analyze_crop_image(image_path: str, crop_type: str = "unknown") -> str:
    """Analyze a crop image for health issues using a VLM."""
    with open(image_path, "rb") as f:
        image_data = base64.b64encode(f.read()).decode("utf-8")

    response = client.chat.completions.create(
        model="gpt-4o",
        messages=[
            {"role": "system", "content": f"""You are an agricultural AI assistant.
Analyze the provided crop image and report:
1. Identified crop (confirm or correct: {crop_type})
2. Overall health assessment (healthy / mild stress / moderate stress / severe)
3. Visible symptoms (discoloration, spots, wilting, pest damage, etc.)
4. Possible causes (disease, nutrient deficiency, pest, water stress)
5. Recommended actions (treatment, monitoring, soil testing)
Be specific and actionable. Note confidence level for each finding."""},
            {"role": "user", "content": [
                {"type": "text", "text": "Please analyze this crop image."},
                {"type": "image_url", "image_url": {
                    "url": f"data:image/jpeg;base64,{image_data}"
                }}
            ]}
        ],
    )
    return response.choices[0].message.content

# Example usage
report = analyze_crop_image("field_photo.jpg", crop_type="wheat")
print(report)
Code Fragment 24.9.1: Crop health analysis using a vision-language model. The system processes field photographs and returns structured assessments with recommended actions.

Deployment considerations for agriculture: Connectivity is a major constraint. Many farms lack reliable internet, requiring edge deployment or offline-capable models. Inference latency is less critical (farmers can wait seconds for an analysis), but model size must fit mobile devices or edge hardware. Seasonal variability means models must handle crops at all growth stages. Multilingual support is essential for global agricultural markets.

2. Energy and Utilities

The energy sector uses LLMs for predictive maintenance, grid optimization, regulatory compliance, and customer service. Power plants and grid operators generate enormous volumes of sensor data, maintenance logs, and regulatory filings. LLMs excel at extracting actionable insights from these unstructured data sources.

Predictive maintenance combines sensor telemetry with LLM-powered log analysis. Traditional approaches use numerical anomaly detection on sensor streams. LLM-augmented systems additionally process maintenance technician notes, equipment manuals, and historical failure reports to provide richer context for anomaly interpretation. When a vibration sensor triggers an alert, the LLM can search maintenance history for similar patterns, identify the most likely failure mode, and recommend specific inspection procedures.

Regulatory compliance in energy is document-intensive. Utilities must comply with regulations from multiple agencies (FERC, NERC, state PUCs in the United States) across thousands of pages of evolving requirements. RAG-based compliance assistants allow engineers and compliance officers to query regulatory documents in natural language, compare current practices against requirements, and generate compliance reports.

3. Government and Public Sector

Government agencies are deploying LLMs for citizen services, policy analysis, and document processing. The public sector faces unique constraints: transparency requirements, accessibility mandates, data sovereignty concerns, and the need to serve diverse populations with varying levels of digital literacy.

Citizen services chatbots handle common inquiries about benefits, permits, tax filing, and public services. These systems must provide accurate information (incorrect guidance about tax obligations or benefit eligibility can have serious consequences), support multiple languages, meet accessibility standards (WCAG 2.1), and maintain audit trails for accountability. The architecture typically combines RAG over official government documents with strict guardrails that prevent the model from speculating beyond its source material.

Policy analysis applications help legislative staff understand the implications of proposed legislation. An LLM can compare a proposed bill against existing law, identify potential conflicts, summarize the positions of different stakeholders, and estimate the impact on various demographic groups. These tools augment (rather than replace) human policy analysts, providing a first draft that speeds up the analysis process.

4. Insurance

Insurance companies process vast quantities of unstructured documents: claims forms, medical records, police reports, property assessments, and policy documents. LLMs are transforming each stage of the insurance value chain.

Claims processing is the highest-impact use case. An LLM agent can read a claim submission (which may include handwritten notes, photographs, and typed narratives), extract structured data (date of loss, type of damage, estimated amount), cross-reference against the policy terms, flag potential issues (pre-existing conditions, coverage exclusions, policy lapses), and route the claim to the appropriate adjuster with a preliminary assessment. This reduces average processing time from days to minutes for straightforward claims. 

# Insurance claims processing with structured extraction
from openai import OpenAI
from pydantic import BaseModel, Field
import json

client = OpenAI()

class ClaimExtraction(BaseModel):
    claimant_name: str = Field(description="Name of the person filing the claim")
    date_of_loss: str = Field(description="Date the loss occurred (YYYY-MM-DD)")
    loss_type: str = Field(description="Category: auto, property, health, liability")
    description: str = Field(description="Brief description of the incident")
    estimated_amount: float = Field(description="Estimated claim amount in USD")
    flags: list[str] = Field(description="Potential issues: coverage gaps, late filing, etc.")
    recommended_action: str = Field(description="Route to adjuster, auto-approve, or investigate")

def process_claim(claim_text: str, policy_summary: str) -> ClaimExtraction:
    """Extract structured data from an insurance claim."""
    response = client.chat.completions.create(
        model="gpt-4o",
        messages=[
            {"role": "system", "content": f"""You are an insurance claims processor.
Extract structured information from the claim submission.
Cross-reference against the policy summary to identify coverage issues.

Policy Summary:
{policy_summary}

Flag any potential issues: late filing (>30 days), coverage exclusions,
pre-existing conditions, or inconsistencies in the narrative."""},
            {"role": "user", "content": claim_text},
        ],
        response_format={"type": "json_object"},
    )
    data = json.loads(response.choices[0].message.content)
    return ClaimExtraction(**data)

# Example usage
claim = process_claim(
    claim_text="On March 15, 2026, my vehicle was rear-ended at a stoplight...",
    policy_summary="Auto policy, collision coverage, $500 deductible, active since 2024"
)
print(f"Action: {claim.recommended_action}, Flags: {claim.flags}")
Code Fragment 24.9.2: Structured claim extraction with policy cross-referencing. The system extracts claim details, identifies coverage issues, and recommends routing actions.

Fraud detection uses LLMs to identify suspicious patterns in claim narratives. Traditional fraud detection relies on numerical features (claim amount, frequency, timing). LLMs add the ability to detect linguistic patterns associated with fraudulent claims: inconsistencies between the narrative and supporting documents, unusual specificity or vagueness in descriptions, and language patterns that correlate with fabricated accounts. These signals are combined with traditional features in an ensemble model.

5. Media and Entertainment

The media industry uses LLMs for content generation, localization, script analysis, and personalized recommendations. LLMs have become integral to content production workflows at major studios and publishers.

Content localization goes beyond translation. LLMs adapt content for different cultural contexts, adjusting idioms, references, and humor while preserving the original tone and intent. For video content, LLMs generate subtitles, dubbing scripts (adapted for lip-sync timing), and audio descriptions for accessibility. A single piece of content can be localized into dozens of markets simultaneously, reducing the time from weeks to hours.

Script analysis helps studios evaluate screenplays and manuscripts at scale. An LLM can summarize a script, assess pacing and structure, identify potential audience demographics, flag content sensitivity issues, estimate production complexity (number of locations, VFX requirements, cast size), and compare against successful properties in the same genre. This does not replace creative judgment but provides a structured first-pass analysis that helps prioritize the reading queue.

6. Telecommunications

Telecom companies operate complex networks, manage millions of customer relationships, and produce extensive technical documentation. LLMs address pain points across all three areas.

Network troubleshooting is the most technically demanding application. When a network issue occurs, engineers must correlate information from multiple monitoring systems, configuration databases, and incident histories. An LLM-powered troubleshooting agent ingests alerts, searches for similar past incidents, queries network configuration databases, and suggests diagnostic steps in order of likelihood. This reduces mean time to resolution (MTTR) by guiding engineers to the most probable root cause faster.

Customer churn prediction traditionally uses tabular ML models on structured customer data (usage patterns, billing history, service calls). LLMs augment this by analyzing unstructured interactions: call transcripts, chat logs, social media mentions, and support ticket narratives. Sentiment and frustration signals extracted from these conversations improve churn prediction accuracy and, more importantly, identify the specific issues driving churn for individual customers, enabling targeted retention interventions.

7. Real Estate

Real estate combines document-intensive transactions with data-driven valuation. LLMs address both sides: parsing and analyzing legal documents (leases, contracts, zoning regulations) and generating market analysis reports from structured data.

Lease analysis is a high-value application for commercial real estate. A typical commercial lease runs 50 to 200 pages and contains complex provisions for rent escalation, operating expense pass-throughs, tenant improvement allowances, renewal options, and termination clauses. An LLM can extract these key terms, compare them against market benchmarks, identify unusual provisions, and generate a summary that highlights risks and opportunities for the tenant or landlord. What previously required hours of attorney review can be completed in minutes for the initial analysis, with human review focused on the flagged provisions.

Market trend reporting uses LLMs to synthesize data from multiple sources (transaction records, permit filings, economic indicators, demographic data) into narrative market reports. Rather than presenting raw data tables, the LLM generates analysis in the style of professional market research, noting trends, comparing submarkets, and providing forward-looking commentary. This democratizes market intelligence that was previously available only to firms with dedicated research teams.

8. Construction and Architecture

Construction projects involve extensive regulatory compliance, safety requirements, and coordination among multiple parties. LLMs help manage the document complexity and improve safety outcomes.

Building code analysis is a natural fit for RAG-based systems. Building codes are voluminous (the International Building Code alone exceeds 700 pages), vary by jurisdiction, and are frequently updated. Architects and engineers can query a code-indexed RAG system in natural language: "What are the fire separation requirements for a mixed-use building with residential above commercial in Zone R-5?" The system retrieves relevant code sections, interprets them in context, and provides a structured answer with citations.

Safety compliance uses VLMs to analyze construction site photographs for safety violations: missing personal protective equipment, unsecured scaffolding, improper material storage, and blocked emergency exits. The system can process photographs from daily site walks, flag potential violations, and generate compliance reports automatically. 

# Construction site safety analysis with a vision-language model
from openai import OpenAI
import base64, json

client = OpenAI()

def analyze_site_safety(image_path: str) -> dict:
    """Analyze a construction site photo for safety compliance."""
    with open(image_path, "rb") as f:
        image_data = base64.b64encode(f.read()).decode("utf-8")

    response = client.chat.completions.create(
        model="gpt-4o",
        messages=[
            {"role": "system", "content": """You are a construction safety inspector AI.
Analyze the site photograph and identify:
1. PPE compliance (hard hats, vests, safety glasses, steel-toe boots)
2. Fall protection (guardrails, safety nets, harnesses on elevated work)
3. Housekeeping (debris, blocked pathways, improper material storage)
4. Electrical safety (exposed wiring, proximity to power lines)
5. Equipment safety (proper use, maintenance indicators)

For each finding, specify: category, severity (low/medium/high/critical),
location in image, and recommended corrective action.
Return structured JSON."""},
            {"role": "user", "content": [
                {"type": "text", "text": "Analyze this construction site photo."},
                {"type": "image_url", "image_url": {
                    "url": f"data:image/jpeg;base64,{image_data}"
                }}
            ]}
        ],
        response_format={"type": "json_object"},
    )
    return json.loads(response.choices[0].message.content)

findings = analyze_site_safety("site_photo.jpg")
critical = [f for f in findings.get("violations", []) if f["severity"] == "critical"]
print(f"Found {len(findings.get('violations', []))} issues, {len(critical)} critical")
Code Fragment 24.9.3: Construction site safety analysis using a VLM. The system processes site photographs, identifies safety violations by category, and generates corrective action recommendations.

Key Insight

Across all industries, the highest-impact LLM applications share a common pattern: they process unstructured documents that humans currently read manually. Claims forms, maintenance logs, regulatory filings, lease agreements, building codes, and medical records all follow this pattern. The LLM does not replace domain expertise; it accelerates the reading, extraction, and initial analysis steps, freeing human experts to focus on judgment, negotiation, and decision-making. Organizations that identify their highest-volume document processing workflows first will see the fastest returns on LLM investment.

Warning

Industry-specific LLM deployments must account for sector-specific regulations. Healthcare requires HIPAA compliance and potentially FDA clearance. Financial services must address SEC and FINRA requirements for algorithmic trading and advisory. Government deployments face data sovereignty and transparency mandates. Insurance applications must pass fairness and bias audits. Before deploying in any regulated industry, consult with domain compliance experts and build regulatory requirements into the system design from the start, not as an afterthought.

Knowledge Check

1. Why are vision-language models particularly valuable for agriculture compared to traditional computer vision?
Show Answer
Traditional computer vision models classify a fixed set of known conditions and cannot handle novel symptoms. VLMs can describe unfamiliar symptoms in natural language and suggest possible causes even for conditions not in their training data. This is critical in agriculture where new diseases, climate-driven stress patterns, and region-specific conditions frequently arise.
2. How do LLMs augment traditional customer churn prediction in telecommunications?
Show Answer
Traditional churn models use structured data (usage, billing, service calls). LLMs add analysis of unstructured interactions: call transcripts, chat logs, and social media mentions. They extract sentiment and frustration signals that improve prediction accuracy and, more importantly, identify the specific issues driving churn for individual customers, enabling targeted retention efforts.
3. What makes government LLM deployments uniquely challenging compared to private sector?
Show Answer
Government deployments face transparency requirements (citizens must be able to understand and challenge AI decisions), accessibility mandates (WCAG 2.1), data sovereignty concerns (government data may not leave national infrastructure), and the need to serve diverse populations. Incorrect guidance about taxes or benefits can have serious legal consequences, requiring stricter guardrails than typical commercial chatbots.

The highest-impact applications process unstructured documents that humans currently read manually: claims forms, maintenance logs, regulatory filings, lease agreements, building codes, and medical records. The LLM accelerates reading, extraction, and initial analysis, freeing human experts for judgment and decision-making. Organizations that identify their highest-volume document workflows first see the fastest returns.

Key Takeaways

  • Agriculture benefits from VLMs for crop health monitoring and pest identification, with edge deployment as the primary deployment challenge.
  • Energy combines sensor telemetry with LLM-powered log analysis for predictive maintenance and regulatory compliance.
  • Government must balance AI capability with transparency, accessibility, and data sovereignty requirements.
  • Insurance gains the most from claims processing automation, with fraud detection as a high-value secondary application.
  • Media uses LLMs for content localization and script analysis, achieving rapid multi-market adaptation.
  • Telecom applies LLMs to network troubleshooting and churn prediction, reducing MTTR and improving retention.
  • Real estate benefits from lease analysis and market reporting automation, democratizing intelligence previously limited to large firms.
  • Construction uses RAG for building code compliance and VLMs for automated safety monitoring.
  • Document processing is the universal high-impact pattern across all industries.
Research Frontier

Industry-specific foundation models are emerging as an alternative to general-purpose LLMs for regulated sectors. Bloomberg trained BloombergGPT on financial data; similar efforts are underway for legal (Harvey AI), healthcare (Google Med-PaLM), and scientific domains. The research question is whether domain-specific pre-training provides meaningful advantages over general-purpose models with domain-specific prompting and RAG. Early evidence suggests that domain models excel at specialized terminology and reasoning patterns but lag behind frontier general models on broader tasks, making hybrid approaches (general model + domain RAG + domain fine-tune) the most practical current strategy.
