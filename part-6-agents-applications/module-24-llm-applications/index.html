Chapter Overview

Large language models are reshaping virtually every industry. What began as research curiosities have become production tools that write code, analyze financial reports, assist doctors, detect cybersecurity threats, and power educational platforms used by millions. This module surveys the most impactful application domains for LLMs, providing both the technical depth needed to build these systems and the domain awareness required to deploy them responsibly.

The module opens with vibe-coding and AI-assisted software engineering, covering how tools like Cursor, Copilot, and Claude Code are transforming how developers write software. It then explores finance and trading applications, healthcare and biomedical AI, recommendation and search systems, cybersecurity applications, education and creative industries, and finally robotics and scientific discovery. Each section examines the unique challenges, architectures, evaluation methods, and regulatory considerations specific to its domain.

By the end of this module, you will understand how LLMs are applied across major industries, be able to design domain-specific LLM applications with appropriate safeguards, and appreciate the regulatory and ethical considerations that vary by sector.

Practical Example: The Vibe-Coding Experiment That Shipped a Feature in One Afternoon

Who: Priya, a senior backend engineer at a mid-size fintech company.

Situation: Her team needed a new CSV reconciliation endpoint for bank statement imports. The backlog estimated two days of development.

Problem: A critical client integration deadline was 36 hours away, and two other engineers were out sick.

Decision: Priya used Claude Code in agentic mode, describing the requirements in natural language and iterating through three rounds of review and refinement.

Result: The endpoint was production-ready in four hours, including unit tests and documentation. It passed code review with only two minor style changes.

Lesson: AI-assisted coding does not eliminate engineering judgment; it compresses the time between intent and implementation. The engineer still needs to specify requirements clearly and review outputs critically.

Practical Example: When a Medical LLM Almost Gave Dangerous Advice

Who: Dr. Chen, chief medical informatics officer at a regional hospital network.

Situation: The hospital piloted an LLM-based clinical decision support tool that summarized patient histories and suggested differential diagnoses.

Problem: During testing, a nurse noticed the system recommended a standard dosage of a blood thinner for a patient whose chart clearly indicated a severe bleeding disorder. The model had summarized the chart correctly but failed to connect the contraindication.

Dilemma: The tool improved average documentation time by 40%, and most clinicians loved it. Pulling it entirely would frustrate staff and waste six months of integration work.

Decision: Dr. Chen added a mandatory human-in-the-loop review step for any recommendation involving medications, plus a rule-based contraindication checker that ran alongside the LLM output.

Result: The hybrid system caught 100% of contraindication issues in the next three months of testing, while preserving the time savings for non-medication tasks.

Lesson: In healthcare, LLMs should augment clinical judgment rather than replace it. Safety-critical domains require layered safeguards, not blind trust in model outputs.

Every cybersecurity team that deploys an LLM for threat detection eventually discovers the same irony: they now need to protect the AI from the same attacks it was hired to detect.

Practical Example: The Recommendation Engine That Learned Too Well

Who: Tomasz, lead ML engineer at a streaming music platform with 8 million users.

Situation: The team replaced their collaborative filtering system with an LLM-powered conversational recommendation engine that let users describe what they wanted in natural language ("something mellow for a rainy Sunday morning").

Problem: Within two weeks, engagement metrics soared, but user complaints about "repetitive recommendations" tripled. The LLM was anchoring heavily on the most popular tracks matching each description, creating filter bubbles even faster than the old system.

How: Tomasz introduced a diversity penalty into the re-ranking step, ensuring that at least 30% of each recommendation set came from outside the user's recent listening history.

Result: Complaint rates dropped by 60%, and monthly active listening time actually increased by 8% as users discovered more new artists.

Lesson: LLMs can optimize so effectively for stated preferences that they suppress exploration. Recommendation systems need explicit diversity mechanisms, especially when the model is highly capable.

Practical Example: The Legal Team That Automated Contract Review

Who: Sandra, general counsel at a fast-growing SaaS company signing 200+ vendor contracts per quarter.

Situation: Her three-person legal team was drowning in contract review, with an average turnaround time of 11 business days per contract. Sales deals were stalling.

Problem: Hiring additional lawyers was too expensive, and outsourcing raised confidentiality concerns given the sensitive terms in their vendor agreements.

Decision: Sandra deployed a RAG-based contract analysis system that extracted key clauses, flagged deviations from their standard terms, and generated redline suggestions for the most common issues.

Result: Average review time dropped to 3 business days. The system correctly flagged 94% of problematic clauses in a blind test against the senior attorney's reviews. The legal team focused their time on the genuinely complex negotiations.

Lesson: LLM applications shine brightest when they handle the high-volume, pattern-heavy work and free domain experts to concentrate on the cases that truly require human judgment.

The first rule of deploying LLMs in production: your most creative users will find ways to use your application that you never imagined, never intended, and probably never wanted.
