Chapter Overview

This chapter takes you behind the curtain of modern language model development. While the Transformer architecture (Module 04) provides the blueprint, the real story of LLMs is one of scale: billions of parameters trained on trillions of tokens, consuming thousands of GPU hours. Understanding how this process works is essential for anyone building with or reasoning about these systems.

We begin by surveying the landmark models that shaped the field, from BERT to GPT-4 (see also the Modern LLM Landscape in Module 07). We then dissect the pre-training objectives that teach models to understand and generate language. Next, we explore the scaling laws that govern how model performance improves with more compute, data, and parameters, and the data curation pipelines that supply the raw material. We cover the optimization algorithms and distributed training infrastructure that make billion-parameter training feasible (with further inference-time optimization covered in Module 08). Finally, we examine the fascinating theoretical question of how in-context learning actually works inside transformers.

Practical Example: The Chinchilla Moment at a Search Company

Who: Priya, lead ML engineer at a mid-size search startup with a $2M compute budget for pre-training their domain-specific language model.

Situation: The team had been following the prevailing wisdom of 2021: make the model as large as possible. They planned to train a 30B-parameter model on 300B tokens.

Problem: After reading the Chinchilla paper, Priya realized their plan was wildly parameter-heavy relative to their data budget. The model would be undertrained.

Dilemma: Should they stick with the impressive-sounding 30B model, or scale down to a 7B model trained on 1.4T tokens for the same compute cost?

Decision: Priya convinced leadership to adopt the compute-optimal ratio: a smaller model trained on far more data.

How: They built a robust data pipeline to source and clean 1.4T tokens of domain-relevant text. The smaller model also cut inference costs by 4x.

Result: The 7B model outperformed their earlier 30B prototype on every internal benchmark, while serving queries at one-quarter the latency.

Lesson: More parameters are not always better. Compute-optimal training, where model size and data are balanced, consistently delivers stronger results per dollar.

Practical Example: Deduplication Saves a Training Run

Who: Carlos, a data engineer at a health-tech company building a medical Q&A model.

Situation: The team scraped 800GB of medical text from forums, PubMed abstracts, and clinical guidelines to build their training corpus.

Problem: Early training showed the model memorizing long passages verbatim and scoring suspiciously high on certain benchmarks. Investigation revealed that roughly 18% of training documents were near-duplicates.

Dilemma: Removing duplicates would shrink the dataset significantly. The team worried they would not have enough tokens to train effectively.

Decision: Carlos implemented MinHash-based deduplication with a Jaccard threshold of 0.8, removing duplicate and near-duplicate documents.

How: After deduplication, the corpus dropped to 640GB. They supplemented it with curated textbook excerpts and high-quality clinical notes, bringing the total back to 720GB of cleaner data.

Result: The retrained model showed 12% higher accuracy on held-out medical question sets and dramatically reduced verbatim memorization of training data.

Lesson: Data quality trumps data quantity. Deduplication is not just a nice-to-have; it directly improves model generalization and reduces memorization risks. (For related techniques on curating training data at scale, see Module 12: Synthetic Data.)

Practical Example: Choosing a Parallelism Strategy Under Pressure

Who: Yuki, an infrastructure engineer at an AI lab tasked with training a 13B-parameter model on a cluster of 64 A100 GPUs.

Situation: The initial training script used standard DDP (Distributed Data Parallel), which worked fine for their previous 1.3B model.

Problem: At 13B parameters, DDP crashed with out-of-memory errors. Each GPU needed to hold a full copy of the model, the optimizer states, and the gradients, far exceeding 80GB per device.

Dilemma: Should the team invest engineering time in FSDP (Fully Sharded Data Parallel), which required rewriting their training loop, or use tensor parallelism, which demanded careful model surgery?

Decision: Yuki chose FSDP with ZeRO Stage 3 sharding, since it required minimal model code changes and PyTorch supported it natively.

How: She wrapped the model with FSDP, enabled activation checkpointing for the Transformer blocks, and used BF16 mixed precision. The combination reduced per-GPU memory from 120GB (theoretical) to under 40GB.

Result: Training completed in 11 days across the 64-GPU cluster, staying within budget. The team later added pipeline parallelism for their 70B follow-up model.

Lesson: Parallelism strategy depends on model size and available hardware. FSDP is often the pragmatic first step for models between 7B and 30B parameters, while larger models may require combining multiple strategies. For parameter-efficient alternatives, see Module 14: PEFT.

Training a large language model is a lot like baking a wedding cake the size of a car. You need the right recipe (architecture), premium ingredients (curated data), an industrial oven (GPU cluster), and the patience of a saint. Burn it once, and you just wasted enough electricity to power a small town for a week.

Scaling laws are the closest thing AI researchers have to a crystal ball. Plot your loss on a log-log chart, draw a straight line, and whisper to your CFO: "If you just give me 10x more compute, I can tell you exactly how much better it will get." The CFO, understandably, may need a moment.
